## Section 1 — Mole Biology & Behavior

### 1.1 Townsend's Mole (Scapanus townsendii)

The Townsend's mole is the largest mole species in North America and the one most frequently encountered on residential properties in Got Moles' service area.

**Physical:**
- 8-9 inches total, 4-5 oz (130-150g). Dense, compact — heavier than it looks.
- Velvety dark gray/black fur. No natural grain — lays flat in any direction for bidirectional tunnel movement.
- Massively oversized, paddle-shaped front paws turned outward. Extraordinary digging leverage.
- Tiny, concealed eyes — nonfunctional for sight, detect light/dark only. Navigate by touch, vibration, smell.
- Hairless pink snout covered in Eimer's organs — microscopic sensors detecting prey movement and vibrations.
- Short thick tail, mostly hairless. Sensory function when moving backward.

**Habitat:** Pacific coast from SW British Columbia to NW California. In Washington, concentrated in lowlands west of the Cascades. Prefers open areas with moist, well-drained soils rich in organic matter: lawns, golf courses, parks, irrigated fields. Key factor is moisture and earthworm density.

**Tunnel architecture:** Complex multi-level system. Permanent deep tunnels run 6-20 inches below the surface as main highways, used repeatedly day after day. Shallow feeding tunnels run 1-4 inches below — the raised ridges across lawns. Many shallow tunnels are used once. Deep tunnels can reach 10+ feet when passing under roads or foundations.

**🎯 KEY FIELD NOTE — quotable as Spencer practitioner expertise:**
> "Townsend's moles can tunnel up to 18 feet per hour. One mole creates damage that looks like multiple animals. When a customer says 'I must have a dozen,' it's usually 1-2."

### 1.2 Pacific Coast Mole (Scapanus orarius)

Second species in the area. Smaller than Townsend's, broader habitat tolerance.

- 6-7 inches, 2-3 oz (60-80g). Noticeably lighter. Tail proportionally longer (~1/4 of body length).
- Velvety fur, nearly identical appearance to Townsend's.
- Skull relatively narrow and long.
- Habitat: drier, brushier, wooded — broader tolerance than Townsend's. Found in nearly every habitat except wet, swampy ground.
- Smaller, less dramatic mounds. Shallower tunnel systems.
- Eats roughly twice body weight in earthworms daily — 100-150 individual worms per day.
- Has been documented foraging on the surface in wet leaf litter at night — uncommon for Townsend's.

### 1.3 Shrew Mole (Neurotrichus gibbsii) — NEW in v1.1

Smallest mole in North America. Third species in service area, far less common on residential lawns.

- 3-4 inches total, under 1 oz. Roughly half the size of Coast mole.
- Dark brownish-black fur, velvety. Smaller forepaws produce noticeably smaller, less dramatic molehills.
- Relatively long tail, partially scaled and hairy.
- Tiny but slightly more developed eyes than Townsend's.
- Habitat: shady ravines, stream banks, moist second-growth forests, wooded edges. Rarely found in center of open irrigated residential lawns.
- **Frequently active ABOVE GROUND** — especially at night and in wet leaf litter.
- Tunnel systems shallower and less extensive than other moles.
- Molehills small and flat — easy to mistake for vole activity.
- Social: occasionally travels in small groups (unlike solitary Townsend's).
- Eats earthworms, insects, AND plant material — broader diet than other moles.

**Field note:** Often confused with voles — hardest species to catch.

### 1.5 Mole Diet & Feeding Behavior

**Primary food: earthworms.**
- 55-93% of diet across species
- Townsend's eats 60-80% of body weight daily
- Coast mole eats up to twice body weight — roughly 100-150 individual worms per day
- They patrol permanent tunnels continuously and dig new feeding tunnels to explore fresh ground

**Secondary food:** Grubs, beetle larvae, centipedes, millipedes, slugs, snails. Townsend's is the only western species that also eats underground plant material.

**🎯 IMPORTANT DISTINCTION (Spencer field-guide quote):**
> "Moles do NOT eat grass roots, bulbs, or vegetables as primary food. That damage is voles or gophers using mole tunnels. The moles are after worms and grubs, not your plants. The damage is from tunneling."

### 1.6 Tunnel Systems

**Deep runs (permanent) — primary trap target:**
- Main highways 6-20 inches deep
- Used repeatedly for months/years
- Travel between feeding areas, nest, territory
- Follow straight or gently curving paths along physical barriers: foundations, fences, walkways, driveways, garden borders
- Highest-percentage trap locations

**Surface runs (feeding):**
- Raised ridges 1-4 inches deep
- Many used once and abandoned
- Runs along edges or connecting to deep systems may be productive

**Nest chambers:**
- Typically under trees, shrubs, or buildings in well-drained areas
- Heavy mound clusters may indicate a nest
- Females line nests with moss, grass, leaves during breeding

**🎯 Volcano-shaped mounds are dump sites, not living quarters.** Never set traps in mounds. Always 18+ inches away.

### 1.7 Activity Patterns & Seasonal Behavior

**Year-round:** Moles do NOT hibernate. Active 365 days/year in Western Washington.

**Daily:** 4-5 hour active cycles day and night. Peak surface activity early morning and late evening, increasing after rain or irrigation.

**Seasonal:**
- **Spring (Mar-May):** High surface activity. Juveniles disperse. Excellent trapping window.
- **Summer/Peak (Jun-Oct):** Soil dries, moles go deeper. Trap placement must be more precise. Target deep runs near irrigated areas, foundations, shade.
- **Fall (Oct-Nov):** Rains return, surface activity increases. Good trapping window.
- **Winter (Dec-Feb):** Deeper tunnels, still active. Breeding begins. Concentrated in deep runs.

**🎯 Spencer's seasonal insight:**
> "The dry summer fools homeowners. They think the moles left. They didn't — they followed the worms deeper. Come autumn rains, the worms move back up, the moles follow, and the mounds come right back."

### 1.8 Breeding & Dispersal

- Mating: December-March. Males extend tunnels seeking females.
- Gestation: 4-6 weeks.
- Litter: Townsend's avg 3 (1-4). Coast 2-4, increasing with age.
- Birth: March-April. Born naked, blind, with large forelimbs.
- Dispersal: Pups leave nest at 30-36 days (May-June). Establish own territories via new tunnels or above-ground travel.
- Lifespan: ~3 breeding seasons (3-4 years).

**🎯 Critical Spencer insight (quotable):**
> "Juvenile dispersal is the #1 cause of new activity on previously cleared properties. Every spring, neighborhood moles produce pups. Those pups disperse looking for unclaimed territory. Your cleared yard is prime new ground. This is why one-time removal doesn't prevent future mole problems — it solves the current one, but the cycle continues."

### 1.9 Mole vs Vole vs Gopher (summary table)

| Feature | Mole | Vole | Gopher |
|---|---|---|---|
| Type | Insectivore (NOT rodent) | Small rodent | Rodent |
| Size | 6-9" | 4-6" | 6-12" |
| Paws | Large, paddle-shaped | Small, normal | Large, clawed |
| Diet | Worms, grubs, insects | Plants, grass, roots | Roots, tubers |
| Mounds | Round/volcano, no plug | None; surface paths | Crescent w/ plug |

## Section 2 — Property Assessment (Spencer's expert framework, public-safe summaries)

### Reading the yard — what Spencer looks for

**Fresh vs. old activity:**
- Fresh mounds: loose damp soil matching subsurface color. Recent activity.
- Old mounds: dried, crusted, grass growing through. Where moles WERE.
- Fresh ridges: soft springy turf. Grass may be yellowing.
- Old ridges: settled, compacted, regrown. Lower priority.

**Key tunnel patterns:**
- **Edge runs** (foundations, fences, sidewalks): mole highways. Highest priority.
- **Connecting runs**: straight lines between mound clusters. Deep runs.
- **Under-obstacle runs**: activity on both sides of walkway/driveway = tunnel underneath.
- **Meandering runs**: aimless wandering ridges. Exploratory. Lowest priority.

### Soil-specific challenges (quotable as Spencer expertise)

| Soil type | Reality |
|---|---|
| Clay | Easiest to trap in. Holds shape. Highest catch rates. |
| Loam | Standard conditions. |
| Sandy (wet) | Manageable. |
| Sandy (dry) | Most difficult. Trap dam needs reinforcement. |
| Amended garden soil | Very loose. Compact firmly. |
