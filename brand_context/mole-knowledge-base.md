## 1. Mole Species in Western Washington

### 1.1 Townsend's Mole (Scapanus townsendii)

**The primary species Got Moles encounters.** Largest mole in North America.

- **Size:** 8-9 inches, 4-5 oz (130-150g). Dense, compact — heavier than it looks.
- **Fur:** Velvety dark gray/black. No natural grain — lays flat in any direction for bidirectional tunnel movement.
- **Front paws:** Massively oversized, paddle-shaped, turned outward. Extraordinary digging leverage.
- **Eyes:** Tiny, concealed, nonfunctional for sight. Detect light/dark only. Navigate by touch, vibration, smell.
- **Snout:** Hairless, pink, covered in Eimer's organs — microscopic sensors detecting prey movement and vibrations.
- **Tail:** Short, thick, mostly hairless. Sensory function when moving backward.
- **Range:** Pacific coast from SW British Columbia to NW California. In Washington, concentrated in lowlands west of the Cascades.
- **Habitat:** Prefers open areas with moist, well-drained soils rich in organic matter: lawns, golf courses, parks, irrigated fields. Key factor is moisture and earthworm density.
- **Tunnel system:** Complex multi-level system. Permanent deep tunnels run 6-20 inches below the surface as main highways, used repeatedly day after day. Shallow feeding tunnels run 1-4 inches below — the raised ridges across lawns. Deep tunnels can reach 10+ feet when passing under roads or foundations.
- **Mounds:** Volcano-shaped mounds form when moles push excavated soil straight up from deep tunnels. A single mole can produce remarkable numbers of mounds quickly.
- **Key fact:** Can tunnel up to 18 feet per hour. One mole creates damage that looks like multiple animals. When a homeowner thinks "I must have a dozen," it's usually 1-2.

### 1.2 Pacific Coast Mole (Scapanus orarius)

**Second most common species in the service area.** Smaller with broader habitat tolerance.

- **Size:** 6-7 inches, 2-3 oz (60-80g). Noticeably lighter. Tail proportionally longer (~1/4 of body length).
- **Fur:** Uniformly dark gray/black, velvety. Nearly identical to Townsend's in appearance.
- **Skull:** Relatively narrow and long.
- **Habitat:** Broader tolerance than Townsend's. Occupies drier, brushier, and wooded habitats in addition to open lawns. Found in nearly every habitat except wet, swampy ground. More common near wooded properties, drier/sandier soils, and less irrigated yards.
- **Behavioral differences:** Smaller, less dramatic mounds. Shallower, less extensive tunnel systems. Eats roughly twice body weight in earthworms daily. Can operate in tighter, more compacted soils. Has been documented foraging on the surface in wet leaf litter at night.

### 1.3 Shrew Mole (Neurotrichus gibbsii)

**Smallest mole in North America.** Third species in the service area, less common on residential lawns.

- **Size:** 3-4 inches total — roughly half the size of the Coast mole. Under 1 oz.
- **Fur:** Dark brownish-black, velvety. Much smaller profile than other moles.
- **Forepaws:** Smaller than both other species — produces noticeably smaller, less dramatic molehills.
- **Tail:** Relatively long for a mole, partially scaled and hairy.
- **Habitat:** Shady ravines, stream banks, moist second-growth forests, and wooded edges. Rarely found in the center of open, irrigated residential lawns. More likely when property borders woodland, ravines, or has heavy tree cover.
- **Key behavioral differences:**
  - Unlike other moles, frequently active ABOVE GROUND — especially at night and in wet leaf litter.
  - Tunnel systems are shallower and less extensive.
  - Molehills are small and flat — easy to mistake for vole activity.
  - Social: occasionally travels in small groups, unlike the solitary Townsend's.
  - Eats earthworms, insects, and plant material — broader diet than other moles.

## 2. Mole Biology & Behavior

### 2.1 Diet & Feeding

**Primary food: Earthworms (55-93% of diet)**

- Townsend's mole eats 60-80% of body weight daily in earthworms.
- Coast mole eats up to twice body weight — roughly 100-150 individual worms per day.
- Moles patrol permanent tunnels continuously and dig new feeding tunnels to explore fresh ground.

**Secondary food:** Grubs, beetle larvae, centipedes, millipedes, slugs, snails.

**Important distinction:** Townsend's is the only western species that also eats underground plant material. However, moles do NOT eat grass roots, bulbs, or vegetables as primary food. That damage is typically voles or gophers using mole tunnels.

**Key content point:** "The moles are after worms and grubs, not your plants. The damage is from tunneling."

### 2.2 Tunnel Architecture

**Deep runs (permanent highways):**
- Main highways 6-20 inches deep, used repeatedly for months/years.
- Travel between feeding areas, nest, and territory.
- Follow straight or gently curving paths along physical barriers: foundations, fences, walkways, driveways, garden borders.

**Surface runs (feeding tunnels):**
- Raised ridges 1-4 inches deep.
- Many used once and abandoned.
- The visible damage homeowners see across their lawns.

**Nest chambers:**
- Typically under trees, shrubs, or buildings in well-drained areas.
- Heavy mound clusters may indicate a nest.
- Females line nests with moss, grass, and leaves during breeding.

**Mounds:**
- Volcano-shaped mounds = excavated soil pushed straight up from deep tunnels.
- Mounds are just dump sites — the mole is NOT living in the mound.

### 2.3 Senses & Navigation

- Moles are effectively blind — tiny eyes detect light/dark only.
- Navigate by touch, vibration, and smell.
- Snout covered in Eimer's organs — microscopic sensors detecting prey movement and vibrations through soil.
- Extremely sensitive to disturbance in their tunnels.
- Fur has no natural grain — allows bidirectional movement through tunnels.

### 2.4 Breeding & Dispersal

- **Mating season:** December-March. Males extend tunnels seeking females.
- **Gestation:** 4-6 weeks.
- **Litter size:** Townsend's averages 3 (range 1-4). Coast mole 2-4, increasing with age.
- **Birth:** March-April. Born naked, blind, with large forelimbs.
- **Dispersal:** Pups leave nest at 30-36 days (May-June). Establish own territories via new tunnels or above-ground travel.
- **Lifespan:** Approximately 3 breeding seasons (3-4 years).
- **Key content point:** Juvenile dispersal is the #1 cause of new activity on previously cleared properties. This is why one-time removal doesn't prevent future mole problems.

## 3. Seasonal Behavior in Western Washington

### Year-Round Activity

**Moles do NOT hibernate.** Active 365 days/year in western Washington.

### Daily Patterns

- 4-5 hour active cycles, day and night.
- Peak surface activity: early morning and late evening.
- Activity increases after rain or irrigation.

### Seasonal Breakdown

**Spring (March-May) — Peak Visibility**
- High surface activity. Soil moisture peaks, forcing earthworms toward the surface — moles follow their food.
- Breeding season drives young moles to seek new territory.
- Busiest season for mole control. Call volume typically doubles between March and May.

**Summer (June-August) — Deeper Activity**
- As soil dries, moles dig deeper. Surface damage often drops.
- Homeowners think moles have left — they haven't.
- Irrigated lawns are the exception: regular watering keeps conditions moles prefer right at the surface.

**Autumn (September-November) — Second Peak**
- Autumn rains return, earthworms move toward the surface, moles follow.
- Fresh mounds reappear — often in areas that looked clear all summer.
- Young moles from spring breeding cycle are fully grown and staking out territories.

**Winter (December-February) — Still Active**
- Moles stay active through Western Washington's mild winters.
- Tunnel below the frost line and keep feeding.
- Breeding begins December-March.
- In mild, wet winters, mole damage can be visible year-round.

| Size | 6-9" | 4-6" | 6-12" |

## 5. Common Myths & Misconceptions

### Myth: Castor oil repellents work
**Reality:** Washington State University's Extension Service notes that repellents like castor oil are "not consistently effective" against moles. They sometimes push moles to a different part of the yard. The moles don't leave the property.

### Myth: Kill the grubs and moles will leave
**Reality:** Moles primarily eat earthworms, not grubs. Earthworms make up 55-93% of a mole's diet. Killing grubs won't meaningfully reduce the food supply. A healthy lawn with good earthworm populations will always attract moles.

### Myth: Sonic/vibration devices repel moles
**Reality:** No scientific evidence supports the effectiveness of sonic or vibration devices for mole control. Moles adapt quickly to consistent vibrations.

### Myth: Flooding tunnels drives moles away
**Reality:** Moles can survive in waterlogged tunnels. They may temporarily relocate within the property but don't leave permanently.

### Myth: Moles hibernate in winter
**Reality:** Moles are active 365 days per year in Western Washington. They tunnel deeper when soil is dry or frozen but never go dormant.

### Myth: Lots of mounds = lots of moles
**Reality:** A single Townsend's mole can tunnel up to 18 feet per hour. One mole creates damage that looks like multiple animals. When a homeowner sees mounds across their entire yard, it's usually 1-2 moles.

### Myth: Once you remove moles, they won't come back
**Reality:** A cleared yard is still attractive territory. Moist soil, healthy earthworm populations, and available tunnels invite neighboring moles. Re-invasion commonly occurs within 3-12 months, especially during spring dispersal and autumn rain return.

### Myth: Moles eat plant roots and bulbs
**Reality:** Moles are insectivores — they eat earthworms, grubs, and insects. They do NOT eat grass roots, bulbs, or vegetables. Plant damage is typically caused by voles or gophers that use mole tunnels for access.

### Myth: Mole poison is safe to use in yards with pets
**Reality:** Most mole poison baits contain bromethalin (a neurotoxin with no antidote) or zinc phosphide (produces toxic phosphine gas). Both are dangerous to dogs through direct ingestion or secondary poisoning from eating a poisoned mole.

## 6. Property & Lawn Impact

### Types of Mole Damage
- **Surface tunnels:** Raised ridges across lawns that kill grass by separating roots from soil.
- **Mounds:** Volcano-shaped soil piles that smother grass and create uneven terrain.
- **Root disruption:** Tunneling near plants can disturb root systems, causing wilting or death — not from eating, but from physical disruption.
- **Aesthetic damage:** A mole-damaged lawn looks neglected. Mounds, ridges, and dead patches affect curb appeal and property value.
- **Trip hazards:** Soft, collapsed tunnels create uneven ground. Risk for people, pets, and lawn equipment.

### Soil Types in Western Washington

| Soil Type | Mole Impact |
|-----------|------------|
| Clay | Common in lowlands. Holds moisture well. Townsend's moles thrive. |
| Loam | Ideal mole habitat. Rich in earthworms. Standard conditions. |
| Sandy (wet) | Manageable for moles. Common near waterways. |
| Sandy (dry) | More difficult for moles. Less common on irrigated properties. |
| Amended/garden soil | Very loose. Easy for moles to tunnel through rapidly. |

### What Attracts Moles to a Property
- Moist, well-drained soil rich in organic matter
- Healthy earthworm populations (sign of a healthy lawn)
- Regular irrigation
- Proximity to wooded areas, parks, or undeveloped land
- Well-maintained turf (ironically, the better your lawn, the more attractive it is to moles)

- Professional mole control involves reading tunnel patterns, identifying active vs. abandoned runs, and strategic placement in high-traffic tunnels.
- Effective trapping depends on precise placement that requires years of field experience.

## 8. Pet Safety & Poison Dangers

### Common Mole Poisons and Their Risks

**Bromethalin (found in Talpirid and similar products):**
- Neurotoxin that causes brain swelling.
- There is NO antidote.
- Dogs can develop tremors, seizures, paralysis, and fatal brain damage.

**Zinc phosphide:**
- Reacts with stomach acid to produce phosphine gas, toxic to all mammals.
- Causes vomiting, abdominal pain, seizures, and organ failure.

**Anticoagulants:**
- Prevent blood clotting.
- Cause internal bleeding.

### How Dogs Get Exposed
1. **Direct ingestion** — dogs dig up and eat the bait itself.
2. **Secondary poisoning** — dogs eat a dead or dying mole that consumed poison.
3. **Soil contact** — some products dissolve into the soil.

### Emergency Response
1. Call vet or ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center immediately — (888) 426-4435
2. Do not wait for symptoms — some poisons cause irreversible damage before symptoms appear.
3. Bring the product packaging if possible.
4. Do not induce vomiting unless directed by a veterinarian.
