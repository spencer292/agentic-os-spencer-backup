# Carousel First Slide Copywriting
## Structures, Formulas and Hooks — Universal Niche Guide

Read this document before drafting the hook for slide 1. Apply one of the 5 base formulas below to the post's content. Validate the result with the checklist at the end.

---

## How to use this document

This guide documents the copy structures that stop the scroll on carousel first slides. Each structure is presented in a universal format — with no fixed niche examples — so you can fill it with your own market reality.

The core logic behind every first slide is:

> **Create an information gap that only closes by swiping.**

The title does not deliver the answer. It delivers the size of the answer.

---

## Dominant Structural Device

### The colon as a reveal

The most used mechanism. Splits the title into two layers:

```
[SHOCKING FACT / STATUS] : [HOW / WHY / promised revelation]
```

The first half stops the scroll. The second justifies the click.

**Why it works:** the brain processes both parts in distinct sequences. The first activates surprise. The second activates promise. Together, they eliminate the objection of "I don't have time for this right now."

## The 5 Base Formulas

### Formula 1 — Value Inversion

Takes an object with a negative charge (problem, addiction, weakness, failure) and reveals it transformed into something desirable.

```
[NEGATIVELY CHARGED OBJECT] BECAME [STATUS / ADVANTAGE / POSITIVE OUTCOME]
```

**Accepted variations:**
- `[IGNORED PROBLEM] BECAME [BIGGEST MARKET OPPORTUNITY]`
- `[ADMITTED WEAKNESS] BECAME [COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE]`
- `[CRITICIZED BEHAVIOR] BECAME [TREND]`

**What must be present:** the tension between what the reader feels about object A and what the title says it became. The greater the contrast, the stronger the hook.

---

### Formula 2 — Numeric Absurdity

Uses two numbers — one ridiculously small and one ridiculously large — separated by a time marker or consequence.

```
[PERSON / COMPANY] [DID / SOLD / LOST] [UNDERESTIMATED VALUE]. [TODAY / RESULT] [ASTRONOMICAL NUMBER]
```

**Accepted variations:**
- `[SMALL INVESTMENT] IN [YEAR]. TODAY IT WOULD BE WORTH [ABSURD NUMBER]`
- `[COMPANY] STARTED WITH [MINIMAL RESOURCE]. NOW MAKES [NUMBER] A YEAR`
- `HE GAVE UP [VALUE X]. THAT DECISION COST [VALUE Y]`

**What must be present:** the period between the two numbers. It forces a dramatic pause — the reader processes the first number, then gets blindsided by the second.

---

### Formula 3 — Common Sense Betrayal

Presents an action that seemed obvious to interpret. Then reveals that everyone interpreted it wrong.

```
[ACTION / DECISION]. EVERYONE THOUGHT IT WAS [WRONG INTERPRETATION]
```

**Accepted variations:**
- `[COMPANY] MADE [MOVE]. THE ENTIRE MARKET THOUGHT IT WAS [MISTAKE]. IT WASN'T`
- `[PERSON] CHOSE [COUNTERINTUITIVE PATH]. EVERYONE DOUBTED`
- `[APPARENT RESULT]. THE REAL STORY IS DIFFERENT`

**What must be present:** the reader must have held the same wrong interpretation as "everyone." If they never thought that, the hook does not work. Validate before using.

---

### Formula 4 — Threatened Identity

Places the reader as a member of a group whose territory, advantage, or right is being taken by an external agent.

```
[TERRITORY / SPACE / MARKET] TAKEN BY [EXTERNAL AGENT]:
HOW [READER'S GROUP] IS BEING PUSHED OUT OF THEIR OWN [DOMAIN]
```

**Accepted variations:**
- `[PROFESSION] BEING REPLACED: WHAT [GROUP] ISN'T SEEING`
- `[MARKET] BECAME [EXTERNAL AGENT]'S TERRITORY. [LOCAL GROUP] LOSING GROUND`
- `THOSE WHO DOMINATED [AREA] FOR YEARS ARE BEING PUSHED OUT`

**What must be present:** the word "their own" or equivalent — it transforms economic disadvantage into an identity affront. That is the real trigger.

---

### Formula 5 — Scandal with Cast and Number

Names recognizable people + a count of others involved + a compromised financial figure. The combination of real people and concrete numbers makes the scandal impossible to ignore.

```
[AUTHORITY] [ARRESTED / FINED / EXPOSED] [NAME 1], [NAME 2] AND [N] OTHERS.
[VALUE] IN [NATURE OF WRONGDOING / IMPACT]
```

**Accepted variations:**
- `[PLATFORM] REMOVED [NAME] AND [N] OTHER ACCOUNTS. [NUMBER] FOLLOWERS AFFECTED`
- `[INVESTIGATION] EXPOSED [NAME], [NAME] AND [N] MORE. [VALUE] AT STAKE`
- `[COMPANY] COLLAPSED. [N] PARTNERS INDICTED. [VALUE] GONE`

**What must be present:** at least one name the reader recognizes and a financial figure with clear magnitude ($, %). Without both, it becomes a generic notice.

## The 3 Cognitive Mechanisms

Every formula above triggers at least one of these mechanisms. Understanding them allows you to create variations not documented here.

### 1. Curiosity Gap

The title delivers the result but hides the path. The brain enters a state of cognitive tension until the gap is filled.

**Sign it's working:** the reader can describe what the carousel promises but cannot guess the content without reading it.

**Common mistake:** revealing too much in the title. If the answer is in the hook, there is no reason to swipe.

---

### 2. Expectation Inversion

The reader holds a consolidated belief. The title directly challenges it. The ego gets involved: who was right?

**Sign it's working:** the reader's first reaction is disagreement or skepticism — not indifference.

**Common mistake:** inverting a belief the reader did not have. Without a prior belief, there is no inversion — only confusion.

---

### 3. World Collision

Combines two semantic universes that should not coexist. The strangeness is almost physical — the brain cannot ignore the impossible combination.

**Sign it's working:** the reader feels the title "makes no sense" but needs to find out how it does.

**Common mistake:** weak collisions where the worlds are too close. The tension needs to be genuine, not merely unusual.

## Technical Elements That Amplify Any Formula

### All caps

Works as an urgency headline — tabloid newspaper, WhatsApp alert, breaking news. Signals "this cannot wait." Use deliberately: in excess, it loses force. On carousel first slides, it is almost always justified.

### Period in the middle of the sentence

Creates a dramatic pause before the second information hit. The reader processes the first block, then gets blindsided by the second.

```
[FACT A]. [CONSEQUENCE B THAT CONTRADICTS A]
```

### Broken numbers

"$1.6 billion" is more convincing than "$2 billion." Broken numbers signal real data, not an estimate.

### Present continuous verb for trends

"Is stopping," "is being redesigned," "is being pushed out" — creates urgency of an ongoing phenomenon. The reader feels they are arriving late if they do not read now.

### Superlative anchored in external authority

"First in the country on Forbes," "Top 3 worldwide," "Guinness record." The superlative alone is empty. Anchored to an external institution, it becomes a verifiable fact.

### `<mark>` word emphasis on the cover

On the `photo-overlay-front` template (and any hook template with a full-bleed image), you can wrap 1–2 critical words in `<mark>...</mark>` tags to render them in the brand's accent color against the white text. Use on the one word that carries the emotional punch of the hook. Example: `"My <mark>$0</mark> AI agent stack: full autonomy in 48 hours."` The marked word is the unexpected element — it is the cognitive entry point.

## Validation Checklist — Before Publishing

Before using any title, run through the questions below:

- [ ] Can the reader describe what the carousel promises without being able to guess the content?
- [ ] Does the title deliver the result but hide the path?
- [ ] Is there at least one element of surprise, tension, or contradiction?
- [ ] If the title uses numbers, are they specific and verifiable?
- [ ] If it uses expectation inversion, did the reader actually hold that expectation?
- [ ] Does the title work without the account's context — would a stranger understand the hook?
- [ ] Is there a clear reason to swipe rather than just like the title?
- [ ] **Is every word in the title understood by your widest reader?** Insider jargon, dev-speak, or industry shorthand in the hook kills reach — the reader scrolls past before parsing. If a non-specialist friend would pause and ask "what does X mean here?", replace the term with a universally-understood equivalent. Examples of jargon that killed hooks in prior runs: "4 ship" (dev-speak for "4 produce usable output"), "TAM" (acronym), "PMF" (acronym), "vibe coding" (insider term). Reserve these for slide bodies where there's room to contextualize.

## Mistakes That Kill the Hook

| Mistake | Why it kills | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Revealing the answer in the title | Eliminates the curiosity gap | Deliver the result, not the path |
| All caps on everything, always | Loses urgency through repetition | Reserve for the central hook element |
| Superlative without an anchor | Becomes self-proclamation, not fact | Attribute to an external source ("according to X") |
| Inverting a belief the reader did not hold | Creates confusion, not tension | Validate whether the belief exists first |
| Round numbers | Read as estimates, not real data | Prefer broken numbers |
| Two hooks in the same title | Dilutes the impact of both | Choose one mechanism per title |
| Insider jargon or dev-speak in the hook | Stops the scroll for the wrong reason — reader doesn't parse it in time | Replace with a universally-understood equivalent ("ship" → "make the cut" / "actually work"; "PMF" → "product fit"; never assume the reader's dictionary matches yours) |
