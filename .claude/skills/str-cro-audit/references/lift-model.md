# LIFT Model Scoring Reference

The LIFT model (WiderFunnel) measures six conversion factors. Each factor gets a 1-10 score
based on observable evidence. No aesthetic opinions. Every score needs proof.

---

## Value Proposition (30% weight)

The single biggest conversion lever. What the visitor gets and why it matters.

### Scoring Rubric

**Score 1-3: Weak**
- Generic or missing value prop. Visitor cannot articulate what you offer after 5 seconds.
- Headlines like "Welcome to our website" or "We help businesses grow."
- Feature dumps without benefit translation.
- No clear audience. Could be for anyone.
- Company-centric language ("We are..." "Our mission...") instead of visitor-centric.

**Score 4-6: Present but vague**
- Value prop exists but uses industry jargon or is feature-focused.
- Benefit is implied, not stated directly.
- Audience is broad. "Professionals" or "businesses" instead of a specific person.
- Headline needs the subheadline to make sense.
- Multiple value props competing. Visitor picks none.

**Score 7-8: Clear and specific**
- Benefit-oriented headline. States what the visitor gets.
- Uses audience language (matches ICP vocabulary, not company vocabulary).
- Specific mechanism or method named. Not just "we help" but "how."
- One primary value prop. Supporting points subordinate.
- Passes the "so what?" test. Every claim answers why it matters.

**Score 9-10: Immediately compelling**
- Specific outcome + specific audience + specific mechanism in the headline.
- Passes 5-second test with strangers who have no context.
- Creates an "aha" moment. The visitor sees themselves in the proposition.
- Differentiated. Could not be copy-pasted onto a competitor's site.
- Emotional and logical. Names the feeling AND the result.

### Evidence to collect
- Headline word count and specificity
- Subheadline presence and support role
- Visual reinforcement (does the hero image support the proposition?)
- ICP language match (compare against icp.md vocabulary)
- "So what?" test on each claim
- Competitor differentiation (could this headline sit on any competitor's site?)

### Common failures
- Feature-focused headlines ("AI-powered platform for...")
- Missing subheadline leaves the headline unsupported
- Headline too clever. Wit beats clarity. Puns that need context.
- Multiple value props fighting for attention
- Company story in the hero instead of visitor benefit

## Relevance (20% weight)

Does the page match what brought the visitor here?

### Scoring Rubric

**Score 1-3: No match**
- Generic page serving all audiences and all traffic sources.
- Headline does not echo the search query or ad copy that brought them.
- Content addresses the wrong level of awareness.
- Visitor has to work to find what they came for.

**Score 4-6: Partial match**
- Right topic, wrong specificity level for the audience.
- Headline mentions the topic but not the specific angle the visitor searched for.
- Content mixes beginner and advanced material without segmentation.
- Some message match with traffic sources, but not tight.

**Score 7-8: Good match**
- Headline echoes the search query or ad copy directly.
- Content addresses the visitor's stated intent.
- Page serves one audience well rather than multiple audiences poorly.
- Awareness level matches. Problem-aware visitors get problem content. Solution-aware
  visitors get solution content.

**Score 9-10: Perfect match**
- Segmented landing pages per traffic source.
- Dynamic content based on referrer or UTM parameters.
- Headline is a mirror of what brought them. Zero cognitive gap.
- Content progression matches their awareness level exactly.

### Evidence to collect
- Headline mirrors likely search terms
- Content addresses stated intent
- Page serves one audience (check for mixed messaging)
- Awareness level alignment (problem/solution/product aware)
- Message match between ads/search and landing page

### Common failures
- One generic page for all traffic sources
- Mismatch between ad promise and page delivery
- Homepage as landing page for paid traffic
- Content written for the company, not the visitor's intent

## Clarity (20% weight)

Can the visitor understand everything without effort?

### Scoring Rubric

**Score 1-3: Confusing**
- No visual hierarchy. Everything looks equally important.
- Walls of text. No scannable structure.
- Jargon-heavy. Requires domain knowledge to understand.
- Multiple competing messages per section.
- Layout feels chaotic. Eye has no natural path.

**Score 4-6: Readable but not scannable**
- Some hierarchy but inconsistent. Heading sizes jump around.
- Copy is readable in isolation but sections blur together.
- Mixed messages. Each section tries to do too many jobs.
- Visual hierarchy exists but has competing focal points.

**Score 7-8: Clear and scannable**
- Logical heading hierarchy (h1 to h2 to h3).
- Scannable copy. Bullets, bold key phrases, short paragraphs.
- One message per section. Each section has one job.
- Plain language. 5th-7th grade reading level.
- Visual hierarchy guides the eye from headline to CTA.

**Score 9-10: Instantly clear**
- Every element has exactly one job. Nothing is ambiguous.
- Copy at 5th-7th grade reading level throughout.
- Visual hierarchy is so clear you could describe the page's purpose from a blurred
  screenshot (squint test).
- Information architecture is invisible. It just works.

### Evidence to collect
- Heading hierarchy (h1 to h2 to h3, logical progression)
- Content scanability (bullets, bold, short paragraphs)
- Reading level (use Flesch-Kincaid or similar estimate)
- Visual hierarchy (squint test: blur the page, is the hierarchy clear?)
- One idea per section (count messages per section)
- Line length (under 75 characters)

### Common failures
- Walls of text with no visual breaks
- Competing visual weights (two things fighting for attention)
- Clever copy over clear copy
- Industry jargon the ICP does not use
- Heading hierarchy violations (h1 to h3, skipping h2)

## Urgency (10% weight)

Is there a reason to act now instead of later?

### Scoring Rubric

**Score 1-3: No urgency**
- No reason to act now. Visitor can bookmark and forget.
- No acknowledgment of the cost of waiting.
- Content is informational only. No call to decide.

**Score 4-6: Mild urgency**
- Problem articulation creates some internal pressure.
- No time-based motivator or cost-of-inaction framing.
- Urgency is implied, not explicit.

**Score 7-8: Strong urgency**
- Names the cost of inaction specifically ("Every month you wait, you lose X").
- Internal urgency through vivid problem description.
- If external urgency is present (deadline, limited spots), it is authentic.

**Score 9-10: Compelling urgency**
- The cost of waiting is visceral and specific.
- Urgency is honest, not manufactured. The visitor trusts it.
- Both internal (pain) and external (timing) urgency work together.

### Key principle for sceptical audiences
Internal urgency (naming the pain of staying the same) ALWAYS beats countdown timers and
fake scarcity. Sophisticated buyers see through manufactured urgency and it destroys trust.
Score countdown timers and "only X left" claims negatively unless you can verify they are real.

### Common failures
- Fake scarcity (destroys trust with sophisticated audiences)
- Zero urgency. No acknowledgment that the problem has a cost.
- Urgency disconnected from the actual problem
- Countdown timers on evergreen offers

## Anxiety (10% weight)

What fears or doubts does the visitor have, and are they addressed?

### Scoring Rubric

**Score 1-3: Unaddressed anxiety**
- No trust signals anywhere on the page.
- No guarantees or risk reversals.
- No social proof or testimonials.
- Visitor has many unanswered questions about risk.

**Score 4-6: Partially addressed**
- Some trust signals present but poorly placed (footer, not near CTA).
- Generic testimonials ("Great service!" with no name or outcome).
- Trust badges present but not contextually relevant.
- Guarantee exists but buried or vague.

**Score 7-8: Well addressed**
- Trust signals placed at decision points (near CTAs, at pricing).
- Named testimonials with specific outcomes.
- Guarantee is clear, specific, and near the CTA.
- Common objections addressed proactively in the content flow.

**Score 9-10: Completely addressed**
- Every potential objection handled at the moment it arises in the decision flow.
- Proof is specific, verifiable, and placed where doubt occurs.
- Risk reversal is prominent and generous.
- The page feels safer than not buying.

### Evidence to collect
- Trust signal types present (testimonials, logos, badges, guarantees, case studies)
- Placement relative to CTAs (within same viewport?)
- Testimonial specificity (name, role, company, specific outcome, photo)
- Guarantee clarity and prominence
- Number of trust signal types (1-3 is optimal. 7+ hurts credibility by ~8%)

### Common failures
- Trust signals in footer only (nobody scrolls to the footer to find trust)
- Generic trust badges with no context
- Testimonials without names, roles, or specific outcomes
- Too many trust signal types (creates a "protesting too much" effect)
- No guarantee or risk reversal near the CTA

## Distraction (10% weight)

What elements compete with the primary conversion goal?

### Scoring Rubric

**Score 1-3: Highly distracted**
- Multiple competing CTAs of equal visual weight.
- Busy navigation with many options pulling attention.
- Sidebar content competing with main content.
- Social media links pulling visitors off-page.
- Decorative elements with no conversion purpose.

**Score 4-6: Some distractions**
- Primary CTA is identifiable but has competition.
- Navigation is present but not overwhelming.
- Some elements serve no clear conversion purpose.
- Secondary CTAs are almost as prominent as primary.

**Score 7-8: Focused**
- One primary CTA per viewport. Clear visual dominance.
- Secondary CTAs are visually subordinate.
- Navigation is minimal and non-competing.
- Every element can justify its presence.

**Score 9-10: Perfectly focused**
- Every element serves the conversion goal.
- Nothing can be removed without losing something important.
- Visual attention flows naturally to the primary action.
- The page is a funnel, not a menu.

### Evidence to collect
- CTA count per viewport (how many things say "click me"?)
- Navigation complexity (item count, dropdown depth)
- Competing links (social media, external links, secondary offers)
- Visual noise (decorative elements, animations, auto-play)
- Attention ratio (number of links vs number of conversion goals. Ideal: 1:1)

### Common failures
- Social media icons in the header competing with CTA
- Multiple "equal" CTAs ("Buy now" and "Learn more" and "Contact us" all same size)
- Decorative animations that draw the eye away from the CTA
- Exit links (social profiles, partner logos that link out)
- Navigation that offers too many choices on a landing page

## PXL Prioritisation Framework

After scoring, prioritise recommendations using these criteria:

| Criterion | Higher priority if... |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Above the fold | The issue is visible without scrolling |
| User complaint | Based on known user feedback, not just heuristic analysis |
| Research-backed | Supported by conversion research data, not opinion |
| Ease of implementation | Can be fixed in under 1 hour vs requires rebuild |
| Impact potential | Affects a high-traffic or high-intent part of the page |

### Recommendation categories

- **Fix now** — Clear problem with a clear solution. Low effort, high confidence.
  Example: "CTA says 'Submit'. Change to 'Get your free audit'."

- **Test** — Strong hypothesis based on evidence. Needs A/B validation before committing.
  Example: "Moving social proof from footer to above-fold could lift conversions."

- **Investigate** — Problem identified but the right solution is unclear. Needs data.
  Example: "Bounce rate is high but unclear if it's message match or page speed."

- **Monitor** — Potential issue. Not enough evidence to act yet. Watch the data.
  Example: "Form has 5 fields. Might be too many, but need form analytics to confirm."
