## Scissor Traps: The Workhorse

The Victor Out O'Sight scissor trap is the most widely used professional mole trap in the Pacific Northwest. There's a reason. It works in the widest range of soil types, sets below the surface where pets and kids can't reach it, and delivers consistent results when placed correctly.

The scissor trap sits inside the tunnel itself. When a mole passes through and pushes against the trigger plate, the jaws close. The design forces precise placement. You have to open the tunnel, position the trap at the right depth, and rebuild the tunnel around it. That's both the strength and the weakness. Done right, it's deadly effective. Done wrong, the mole detects the disturbance and reroutes.

**Pros:**
- Works in clay, loam, and amended soils common across Western Washington
- Below-ground placement keeps it hidden and safe
- Durable. A well-maintained scissor trap lasts years.
- Affordable at $15-30 per trap

**Cons:**
- Requires digging into the tunnel and rebuilding it around the trap
- Setting the spring takes hand strength and practice
- Moles will avoid a poorly placed trap for weeks

## Harpoon Traps: Easiest for Beginners

The harpoon trap (also called a plunger trap) is the one most people picture when they think of mole traps. Metal spikes, a spring-loaded plunger, set on top of a raised surface tunnel. You step on the tunnel to collapse it, place the trap over the compressed section, and the mole triggers it when it pushes back up to reopen the tunnel.

If you're a homeowner trying DIY mole control for the first time, the harpoon trap is the simplest to set. No digging. No tunnel excavation. You can see whether it's been triggered from across the yard.

**Pros:**
- Easiest trap to learn and set
- No tunnel excavation required
- Visible trigger indicator saves time checking
- Works well in soft, loose soils

**Cons:**
- Only works on shallow surface runs (1-4 inches deep)
- Surface runs are often used once and abandoned. You might be trapping an empty highway.
- Exposed above ground. Not ideal for yards with curious dogs or small children.
- Struggles in hard, compacted, or rocky soil where the spikes can't penetrate

The honest problem with harpoon traps: they target surface tunnels. And surface tunnels are the least reliable place to catch a mole. Many surface runs are feeding tunnels used once and never revisited. A Townsend's mole can dig 18 feet of new tunnel per hour. It may never come back through the run you just trapped.

## Choker Loop Traps: Effective in the Right Hands

Choker loop traps (like the Trapline Mole Trap or Nash Choker Loop) use a wire loop that tightens around the mole as it passes through. These are effective in deeper runs, especially along foundations, fence lines, and walkways where moles travel repeatedly.

Professional trappers often use choker loops for deep-run trapping because the design works well in firm soil where scissor traps can be harder to seat properly.

**Pros:**
- Excellent for deep runs along structures
- Low profile once set
- Works in firm, compacted soil
- Reliable trigger mechanism

**Cons:**
- More finicky to set than scissor or harpoon traps
- Requires accurate identification of active deep runs
- Not widely available in big-box stores

## Mole Bait vs. Mole Traps: Why We Don't Use Poison

Mole baits like Talpirid are shaped like earthworms and laced with bromethalin, a neurotoxin. The idea is that the mole eats the fake worm and dies underground. In practice, results are inconsistent. Moles have highly developed senses of smell and touch. Many detect the artificial bait and ignore it.

And mole poisons create real danger for pets.

**Bromethalin has no antidote.** If a dog digs up and eats a bait worm, or eats a poisoned mole, the result can be seizures, paralysis, and fatal brain swelling. Zinc phosphide, another common mole poison, produces toxic gas in the stomach.

If you have pets and you're considering mole bait, don't. The risk-reward ratio doesn't make sense when traps are more effective and completely nontoxic.

## Why Placement Beats the Trap Every Time

Most "best mole trap" reviews won't tell you this: the trap you choose matters far less than where you put it.

Four well-placed traps will outperform ten scattered across random surface tunnels. Every time. The difference between a successful trap set and one that sits untouched for weeks comes down to tunnel selection, depth, and scent:

**1. Tunnel selection.** Deep runs along foundations, fences, driveways, and garden borders are high-traffic highways moles use daily. Surface runs across the middle of your lawn are often used once. Trap the highways, not the side streets.

**2. Placement depth.** A trap set too shallow or too deep won't trigger. Deep runs in Western Washington soils typically sit 6-20 inches below the surface. You need to find the actual tunnel depth at your specific set location.

**3. Scent control.** Moles are extremely sensitive to disturbance. A trap that smells like human hands, has soil packed around it incorrectly, or disrupts the tunnel walls will get avoided. Experienced trappers handle traps with gloves and rebuild tunnel sections to feel natural.

**Never set a trap in a mound.** Mounds are just dump sites where moles push excavated dirt to the surface. The mole is not living in the mound. Always set traps at least 18 inches away from any mound, in a confirmed active tunnel run.

## How Many Traps Do You Actually Need?

Most homeowners think they need a dozen traps because it looks like a dozen moles are tearing up their yard. It's not.

A single Townsend's mole tunnels up to 18 feet per hour and can create damage across an entire property in days. When you see mounds everywhere, it's usually 1-2 moles. Not ten.

For a typical residential property in the Puget Sound area, 3-5 strategically placed traps in confirmed active deep runs is the right starting point. More traps in the wrong locations won't help. Fewer traps in the right locations will.

## When DIY Trapping Works (and When It Doesn't)

We'll be straight with you. DIY mole trapping can work. If you have a single mole, you can identify the active deep run, you set a scissor or harpoon trap correctly, and you check and adjust it regularly, you may catch it.

Where DIY falls short:

- **Reading tunnel patterns.** Knowing which runs are active deep highways vs. abandoned feeding tunnels takes experience. A lot of it.
- **Adjusting after failed sets.** Moles respond to trapping attempts. They reroute, dig deeper, shift to a different part of the yard. A professional adjusts. Most homeowners set a trap and wait.
- **Persistent re-invasion.** You catch one mole. Two months later, a new one moves in through the same tunnel system. Without ongoing monitoring, you're back to square one every season.

Professional catch rates vastly exceed DIY because of experience reading tunnel patterns and adapting to mole behavior in real time. That's not a sales pitch. It's 15+ years of data across nearly 5,000 properties.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best mole trap for beginners?

The harpoon (plunger) trap is the easiest to set because it requires no tunnel excavation. Place it over a collapsed surface run and wait for the mole to push back up. However, surface runs are often abandoned, so success rates are lower than with scissor traps placed in active deep runs. If you're willing to learn proper tunnel prep, a Victor scissor trap will deliver better results.

### Are mole traps safe for pets and children?

Underground traps like scissor and choker loop models are the safest because they sit below the surface where pets and children can't access them. Harpoon traps sit above ground and have exposed spikes, so keep curious dogs away. Mole poison baits are the biggest danger. Bromethalin has no antidote and can be fatal to dogs through direct ingestion or secondary poisoning.

### Should I use mole bait or mole traps?

Traps. Mole baits are inconsistent because moles often detect and avoid artificial worm-shaped baits. Worse, baits containing bromethalin or zinc phosphide pose serious risks to pets. Traps are more effective, reusable, and completely nontoxic. Got Moles uses only chemical-free trapping methods for exactly this reason.

### How many moles are actually in my yard?

Probably 1-2. A single Townsend's mole can tunnel 18 feet per hour and create mounds across an entire property in a matter of days. The damage looks like a dozen animals, but moles are territorial and solitary. In most residential yards in Western Washington, 1-2 moles cause all the visible damage.

### Why do my mole traps keep failing?

The three most common reasons: wrong tunnel (you're trapping an abandoned surface run instead of an active deep highway), wrong depth (the trap isn't seated at the actual tunnel level), or human scent on the trap and surrounding soil. Moles are extremely sensitive to disturbance. If a trap sits untriggered for more than a week, move it to a deep run along a foundation, fence line, or walkway. These structural runs are used daily and give you the highest probability of a catch.
