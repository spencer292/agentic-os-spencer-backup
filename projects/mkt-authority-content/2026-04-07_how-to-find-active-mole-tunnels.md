# How to Find Active Mole Tunnels: A Step-by-Step Guide

To find active mole tunnels, press down a short section of a raised tunnel with your foot, mark it with a flag, and check back in 24-48 hours. If the tunnel is pushed back up, a mole is actively using that run. If it stays flat, the tunnel has been abandoned. Focus your efforts — including trap placement — on confirmed active runs, especially deep runs along foundations, fences, and walkways.

## Why Finding Active Tunnels Matters

A single mole can dig 100 feet of new tunnel in a day. Over weeks, your yard accumulates a network of runs — some used daily, some used once and never touched again.

Here's the problem: most of those tunnels are dead. If you set a trap in an abandoned run, it'll sit there for weeks and catch nothing. The mole walked through that tunnel once, grabbed a worm, and moved on. It has no reason to come back.

Finding the active runs is the most important step in mole control. Get this wrong, and everything that follows is wasted effort.

## Types of Mole Tunnels

Not all mole tunnels are the same. Moles create two distinct tunnel types, and if you don't know the difference, you'll waste time targeting the wrong ones.

### Deep Runs (Main Highways)

Deep runs are the permanent tunnels — the mole's highway system. These sit 6 to 20 inches below the surface. You can't see them from above because they don't raise the turf.

Deep runs follow structure. Look for them along:

- **Foundations** — house, garage, shed
- **Fences and property lines**
- **Walkways, driveways, and patios**
- **Straight lines between mound clusters**

These are the most valuable tunnels to find. A mole uses the same deep run for months or even years. It's how it travels between feeding areas, its nest, and the rest of its territory. Set a trap in an active deep run and you're placing it on a road the mole travels every day.

**How to identify deep runs:** You need a probe rod (a thin metal rod, about 1/4-inch diameter). Push it into the ground along edges — near your foundation, along a fence line, between two clusters of mounds. When the rod drops suddenly into a void, you've found a tunnel. Mark it.

If you find a tunnel on both sides of a walkway or driveway, there's almost certainly a deep run connecting them underneath.

### Surface Runs (Feeding Tunnels)

Surface runs are the raised ridges you see across your lawn — the classic sign of mole activity. These sit just 1 to 4 inches below the surface. The mole is hunting for earthworms and insect larvae near the root zone.

The catch: many surface runs are used once and abandoned. A mole pushed through the soil, ate what it found, and kept going. It has no reason to return to that specific ridge.

Surface runs worth targeting:

- **Runs that connect to a deep run** — these are on-ramps to the highway
- **Runs along an edge** (fence line, garden border, sidewalk) — moles follow edges
- **Straight, direct runs** between two points — likely a travel route

Surface runs to ignore:

- **Meandering, zigzag ridges** in the middle of the lawn — these are exploratory, lowest priority
- **Short dead-end ridges** — the mole was just foraging

### Mole Mounds (Molehills)

The volcano-shaped mounds of dirt are not tunnels at all. They're dump sites. When a mole digs a deep run, it pushes the excavated soil straight up to the surface, creating a mound.

**Never set a trap in a mound.** The mole pushed soil up through a vertical shaft and is long gone. Always place traps at least 18 inches away from a mound, in the horizontal run that connects to it.

What mounds do tell you:

- **Fresh mounds** (loose, damp soil, no grass growing through) = recent activity nearby
- **Old mounds** (dried out, crusted over, grass growing through) = activity may have moved on
- **Heavy clusters of mounds** in one area = possible nest chamber nearby (moles nest under trees, shrubs, or buildings)

## How to Test if a Mole Tunnel Is Active

This is the step-by-step method professionals use. It's simple, but you need patience — rushing it gives you bad data.

### What You'll Need

- Small landscape flags or wooden stakes (to mark test spots)
- A probe rod (for locating deep runs — a length of 1/4-inch rebar or a commercially available mole probe works)
- A notebook or phone camera to track results
- 24-48 hours of patience

### Step 1: Identify Candidate Tunnels

Walk your property and identify every tunnel type you can see or probe:

1. **Probe along edges first.** Walk along your foundation, fence lines, walkways, and driveways. Push the probe rod into the ground every 12 inches. When it drops into a void with little resistance, mark the spot. These are your highest-priority deep runs.
2. **Map surface ridges.** Look for raised ridges across the lawn. Note which ones run in straight lines versus which ones wander randomly. Prioritize straight runs and runs that connect to edges.
3. **Assess mound freshness.** Check each mound cluster. Fresh mounds (soft, moist soil) tell you there's recent digging nearby. Old mounds (hard, dry, grass growing through) suggest the mole has moved on.

### Step 2: Collapse Test Sections

For each candidate tunnel:

1. **Surface runs:** Step on a 6-inch section of the raised ridge to press it flat. Press firmly — you want to fully collapse the tunnel, not just dent it.
2. **Deep runs:** If you've found a deep run with the probe, push the probe rod in to collapse a small section of the roof. Or dig a small hole to expose the tunnel, then backfill it lightly with loose soil.
3. **Place a flag** at each collapsed section so you can find it again.

Test at least 8-10 spots across the property. More data points give you a clearer picture of where the mole is most active.

### Step 3: Wait 24-48 Hours

This is the hard part. Leave everything alone. Don't walk over the test spots. Don't check after six hours. Give the mole time to make its rounds.

Moles travel their main runs at least once a day — most travel them twice (early morning and evening). If a tunnel is active, the mole will push through the collapsed section and repair it within 24-48 hours.

### Step 4: Read the Results

Check each flagged spot:

- **Tunnel pushed back up or soil displaced** = Active run. This is where you focus.
- **Tunnel still flat, no disturbance** = Inactive. The mole is no longer using this route.
- **Partially repaired** = Possibly active but low-traffic. Test again or monitor.

### Step 5: Prioritize What You Found

Now rank your active tunnels. Not all active runs are equal:

| Priority | Tunnel Type | Why |
|----------|------------|-----|
| **Highest** | Active deep run along a foundation, fence, or edge | Main highway — used daily, connects the entire tunnel system |
| **High** | Active surface run connecting to a deep run | On-ramp to the highway — high daily traffic |
| **Medium** | Active surface run along an edge | Travel route, but may be secondary |
| **Lower** | Active surface run in open lawn, straight | Feeding route — used regularly but less predictable |
| **Lowest** | Active meandering surface run | Exploratory — the mole may not come back here often |

If you're setting traps, start with the highest-priority active runs. A trap in the main highway sees more traffic than a trap in a random feeding tunnel.

## Tips for Better Results

- **Test after rain.** Moles are most active when the soil is moist because earthworms move closer to the surface. A test done during dry conditions may undercount active runs.
- **Retest monthly.** Tunnel networks change. A mole that was using the north side of your yard in March may shift south by May as food sources move.
- **Look for the nest.** Heavy mound clusters near a tree, shrub, or building often indicate a nest chamber. Deep runs radiating out from this area are almost always active.
- **Two sides of a hard surface = tunnel underneath.** If you find active tunnels on both sides of a walkway, driveway, or patio, there's a connecting run beneath it. You can't trap under concrete, but you can trap the runs on either side.
- **Morning inspections are best.** Check your test spots early in the morning. Moles are most active in the early hours, so fresh repairs are easiest to spot before foot traffic or weather disturbs the evidence.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I tell if a mole tunnel is active or abandoned?

Press down a section of the tunnel and check back in 24-48 hours. If the tunnel is pushed back up, a mole is actively using it. If it stays flat after 48 hours, the tunnel is abandoned. Active surface runs also tend to have softer, springier soil and possibly yellowing grass, while old runs are compacted with regrown turf.

### What's the difference between a mole runway and a feeding tunnel?

A runway (deep run) is a permanent tunnel 6-20 inches below the surface that moles use as a transportation highway — they travel it daily for months or years. A feeding tunnel (surface run) is a shallow, raised ridge just 1-4 inches deep where the mole hunts for earthworms. Many feeding tunnels are used once and abandoned. Runways are always the higher-priority target.

### Where is the best place to set a mole trap?

Set traps in confirmed active tunnels — specifically, active deep runs along foundations, fences, walkways, or other structural edges. These are the mole's main highways with the highest daily traffic. Never set a trap in a mole mound. Always place traps at least 18 inches away from mounds, in the horizontal tunnel connected to the mound.

### How many active tunnels does one mole create?

A single mole can maintain several hundred feet of permanent deep tunnels and add 100 feet of new surface tunnels in a day. Most of the surface tunnels are temporary feeding runs. The permanent deep run network is smaller but heavily used — and that's where control efforts are most effective.

### Should I collapse all mole tunnels on my property?

Collapsing every visible tunnel isn't productive. It won't drive the mole away — it will just dig new ones. Use selective collapsing as a testing method to identify which tunnels are active. Then focus your control efforts on the confirmed active runs, especially the deep highways.
