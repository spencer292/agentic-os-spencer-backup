# How Long Do Moles Live? Mole Lifespan and What It Means for Your Yard

Moles live approximately 3 to 4 years in the wild, which translates to roughly 3 breeding seasons. That might sound short, but a single mole can tunnel over 100 feet per day and produce a new litter every spring. Over a 3-year lifespan, one mole and its offspring can turn a pristine lawn into a network of raised tunnels and dirt mounds that takes months to repair.

## Why Mole Lifespan Matters More Than You Think

Most homeowners focus on the moles they can see evidence of right now. But the real question is what happens over time. A mole that moves onto your property in its first year has two to three more breeding seasons ahead of it. Each breeding season produces an average litter of 3 pups. Those pups disperse and establish their own territories within weeks.

Understanding how long moles live, how they breed, and how far they travel gives you a much clearer picture of why mole problems seem to come back even after you think the issue is resolved.

## The Mole Life Cycle: Year by Year

A mole's life follows a predictable pattern driven by food, territory, and reproduction.

**Year 1 — Establishment.** A young mole leaves its mother's territory at just 30 to 36 days old, typically in May or June. It disperses across the landscape looking for unoccupied ground with enough earthworms to sustain it. Once it finds suitable territory, it digs an extensive tunnel system. Townsend's mole, the most common species in Western Washington, can tunnel up to 18 feet per hour and over 100 feet in a single day.

**Year 2 — Breeding begins.** Moles breed between December and March. Gestation lasts 4 to 6 weeks, and a litter of 1 to 4 pups (average of 3) is born between March and April. The pups grow quickly and leave the nest by late May or early June to find their own territory.

**Year 3 and beyond.** The mole repeats the breeding cycle. By its third year, it has produced two litters and its territory is well established. Mortality increases from predators, disease, and environmental stress. Most moles do not survive past their fourth year.

## How Far Do Moles Travel?

Moles are not long-distance travelers in the way that deer or raccoons are. But they cover more ground than most people realize.

A single mole's home range is typically one-quarter to one-third of an acre. That is roughly the size of two to three average suburban front yards. Within that territory, a mole maintains a network of deep permanent tunnels (12 to 18 inches below the surface) and shallow feeding tunnels just below the sod.

The real distance issue is juvenile dispersal. When pups leave the nest in May and June, they travel across the surface and through existing tunnel systems looking for open territory. This is the number one cause of new mole activity on properties that were previously cleared. Your neighbor's moles had pups, and one of those pups just moved into your yard.

Moles can also swim for up to an hour, which means streams, ditches, and even small ponds are not barriers to dispersal. If there are earthworms and suitable soil on the other side, a mole can get there.

## Do Moles Live Alone?

Yes, for most of the year. Moles are solitary and territorial. Outside of the December-to-March breeding season, each mole defends its own tunnel system and does not tolerate other moles in its territory.

This is actually useful information for homeowners. If you are seeing mole activity across your entire half-acre lot, it might feel like an invasion. In reality, it could be one or two moles working a large territory. A single Townsend's mole eats 60 to 80 percent of its body weight in earthworms every day, so it needs a substantial foraging area to sustain itself.

The exception is breeding season. Between December and March, males expand their tunneling to find females. You may notice a spike in surface tunnel activity during late winter — that is often males digging exploratory runs to locate a mate.

## Do Moles Come to the Surface?

Rarely. Moles spend almost their entire lives underground. Their bodies are built for it — powerful front claws, tiny eyes, velvety fur that brushes in any direction so they can move forward and backward in tight tunnels.

The one exception in Western Washington is the shrew mole, a smaller species that is frequently active above ground. If you see a small, dark, mouse-sized animal scurrying across your lawn or garden bed, it is likely a shrew mole rather than a Townsend's mole.

Townsend's moles come to the surface mainly during juvenile dispersal. Those 30-to-36-day-old pups leaving the nest in May and June sometimes travel above ground to find new territory. This is one of the few times you might actually see a mole on the surface.

One more thing worth knowing: moles do not hibernate. They are active 365 days a year in Western Washington. Their tunnel activity shifts deeper during cold snaps and closer to the surface during mild, wet weather when earthworms are near the top of the soil. But they never stop.

## What This Means for Your Yard

Here is where mole biology connects to the mole problem in your lawn.

**The comeback factor.** Even after successful trapping, new moles can move in. A cleared property is actually attractive to dispersing juveniles because the tunnel system is already built and the territory is undefended. This is why one-time removal solves the current problem but does not prevent the next one.

**Timing matters.** The worst time to ignore mole activity is late winter through early spring. Breeding season (December through March) is when moles are expanding tunnels, and the pups that follow in March and April are the next generation of yard damage. Addressing mole activity before pups disperse in May reduces the total mole population in your area.

**One mole does a lot of damage.** Because moles are solitary and each one tunnels aggressively to find food, a single mole on a quarter-acre lot can produce dozens of surface tunnels and mounds in a matter of weeks. Homeowners often assume they have five or six moles when the damage is actually coming from one or two.

**Year-round activity means year-round risk.** Moles do not take a season off. In Western Washington's mild, wet climate, earthworms stay near the surface for most of the year. That keeps moles active and tunneling close to where your grass roots are.

Moles in Western Washington typically live 3 to 4 years. The Townsend's mole, the most common species in the region, reaches breeding age in its first winter and produces one litter per year for the remainder of its life. Most moles do not survive past their fourth year due to predation and environmental factors.

### Do moles keep coming back after removal?

Yes, and mole lifespan is a big part of the reason. A single breeding female produces an average of 3 pups per year. Those pups disperse in May and June looking for open territory. A property that was cleared of moles is actually more attractive to juveniles because the tunnel infrastructure is already in place and the territory is undefended.

### How many moles are usually in a yard?

Most residential properties in Western Washington have 1 to 3 moles at any given time. Moles are solitary and territorial, so a quarter-acre lot rarely supports more than one or two adults. The extensive damage pattern often makes homeowners assume there are more moles present than there actually are.

### Are moles active in winter?

Moles do not hibernate and are active every day of the year. In Western Washington's mild climate, moles tunnel year-round. Winter is actually breeding season (December through March), so mole activity often increases during the coldest months as males dig exploratory tunnels to find mates.

A Townsend's mole can tunnel up to 18 feet per hour and over 100 feet in a single day.
