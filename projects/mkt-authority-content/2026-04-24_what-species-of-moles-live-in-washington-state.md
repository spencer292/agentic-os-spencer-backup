# What Species of Moles Live in Washington State? The 3 Native Moles Explained

## BLUF

Three mole species live in Washington State: the **Townsend's mole**, the **Pacific Coast mole**, and the **Shrew mole**. All three are native insectivores, not rodents, and they concentrate in the moist lowlands west of the Cascades. Townsend's is the largest and the one you'll almost certainly find in a damaged lawn. Pacific Coast moles are smaller and range into drier, woodier habitats. Shrew moles are tiny, live mostly in ravines and forest edges, and unusual in that they sometimes forage above ground. Here's how to tell them apart, where each one lives in Washington, and why this state is a mole hotspot.

## The Three Mole Species of Washington State

### 1. Townsend's Mole (*Scapanus townsendii*) — the biggest and most common

The Townsend's mole is the largest mole species in North America. Adults measure 8-9 inches long and weigh 4-5 ounces. Their velvety dark-grey fur lays flat in any direction, which lets them move backward through a tunnel as easily as forward. The paddle-shaped front paws are oversized for the body — built for shifting a remarkable amount of earth.

Townsend's moles live in open, irrigated habitats. Lawns, golf courses, parks, pastures, and agricultural fields — anywhere the soil stays moist and organic matter is plentiful. They are solitary and territorial. Each mole maintains a complex tunnel network with permanent highways 6-20 inches down and shallower feeding runs 1-4 inches below the surface. A single Townsend's mole can tunnel up to 18 feet per hour, which is why homeowners often think they have a dozen when they really have one.

If you're seeing volcano-shaped mounds on your Western Washington lawn, this is almost certainly the species you're dealing with.

### 2. Pacific Coast Mole (*Scapanus orarius*) — the versatile mid-size mole

The Pacific Coast mole is the second most common species in the service area. Smaller and lighter than the Townsend's — 6-7 inches long, 2-3 ounces — with a proportionally longer tail. Fur coloring is nearly identical, which makes physical identification from a photo essentially impossible without a ruler.

The habitat range is what distinguishes this species. Pacific Coast moles tolerate drier soils, brushy ground, and wooded edges that a Townsend's would avoid. They're at home in yards that border forest, in landscaping with less irrigation, and in the transition zone between residential and undeveloped land. They've been documented foraging on the surface in wet leaf litter at night — rare for a mole.

Pacific Coast moles eat roughly twice their body weight in earthworms every day and produce smaller, flatter mounds than a Townsend's. The tunnel systems are shallower and less extensive, which sometimes makes them harder to spot until damage has spread.

### 3. Shrew Mole (*Neurotrichus gibbsii*) — the tiny outlier

The Shrew mole is the smallest mole in North America. Adults are 3-4 inches total length and weigh under 1 ounce — roughly half the size of a Pacific Coast mole. Fur is dark brownish-black and velvety.

Shrew moles are unusual for two reasons. First, they often travel in small groups rather than living solitary like the other two species. Second, they spend significant time **above ground** — foraging through wet leaf litter, stream banks, and forest-floor duff, especially at night. Their tunnel systems, when they do tunnel, are shallow and short. Mounds are tiny and flat, easy to confuse with vole surface trails.

This is not the mole chewing up your lawn. Shrew moles are shady-ravine, second-growth-forest, wooded-property animals. If your property borders heavy tree cover or a ravine, you might encounter one. On a standard irrigated residential lawn in central Seattle or Tacoma, you won't.

## Where Each Species Lives in Washington

Washington State sits at the center of the Pacific Northwest mole range. The population density is concentrated west of the Cascades — the low-elevation coastal belt from the Strait of Juan de Fuca south to the Columbia River.

- **Lowland King, Pierce, and Snohomish Counties** are Townsend's mole strongholds. Sammamish, Bellevue, Redmond, Issaquah, Seattle, Tacoma, Puyallup, Federal Way — all of the suburban corridor is prime habitat. Irrigated lawns, healthy earthworm populations, clay and loam soils, year-round moisture.
- **Western slope of the Cascades and the forested foothills** — Pacific Coast moles dominate where landscaping gets drier or more wooded. Enumclaw, Buckley, Black Diamond, North Bend, the eastern edge of Bothell and Woodinville. Both species can overlap here.
- **Olympic Peninsula and the wet coastal lowlands** — both Townsend's and Pacific Coast. Shrew moles are more likely along stream banks and in second-growth forest.
- **East of the Cascades** — mole activity drops off substantially. Eastern Washington's drier climate and different soil profile isn't hospitable to Townsend's moles. Occasional Pacific Coast sightings near irrigated agricultural land, but not the lawn-damage problem it is in the west.

The entire Got Moles service area — Western Washington's residential corridor from Everett south through Olympia — sits inside the overlapping range of Townsend's and Pacific Coast moles. In almost 5,000 service calls since 2017, the species showing up on residential lawns is Townsend's the overwhelming majority of the time.

## How to Tell Which Species Is in Your Yard

Here's a field guide to the evidence, not the animal (since you'll rarely see the mole itself):

| Evidence | Townsend's | Pacific Coast | Shrew |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mound size | Large, volcano-shaped | Smaller, flatter | Very small, flat |
| Mound frequency | Many, clustered | Scattered, fewer | Rare |
| Surface ridges | Prominent raised tunnels | Less pronounced | Minimal |
| Above-ground activity | Never | Occasional (night) | Frequent |
| Habitat | Open irrigated lawn | Mixed lawn/brush | Ravine / forest edge |
| Seasonal pattern | High spring + autumn | Similar, lower amplitude | Least seasonal |
| Field ID rule | >8", heavy, short tail | <7", lighter, longer tail | <4", tiny |

If the yard is an established residential lot with irrigation and mature landscaping, you're looking at Townsend's. If the property borders woods or has drier sections where damage appears, Pacific Coast is a possibility. Shrew moles barely touch residential lawns.

## Why Moles Thrive in Washington

Western Washington is mole paradise for one reason that cascades into everything else: **the ground never dries out**.

Pacific Northwest rainfall keeps soil consistently moist year-round. Moles live where earthworms live, and earthworms concentrate where moisture is reliable. The loamy glacial till and alluvial clay across the Puget Lowlands hold water well, which is why earthworm populations here are dense enough to feed a 4-ounce predator that eats 60-80% of its body weight every day. Between 55% and 93% of a Townsend's mole's diet is earthworms. A lawn on rich Western Washington soil is essentially a buffet.

Add mild winters on top. Moles don't hibernate, and the ground rarely freezes here to a depth that would force them into deep inactivity. Mole activity continues 365 days a year, with small seasonal variations.

That combination means mole pressure here is higher and more persistent than in most of the country. A cleared lawn stays cleared only as long as no new mole disperses in from a neighboring property. And they will — especially during the spring dispersal window (March-May) and the autumn rain return (September-November).

## FAQ

### Are any Washington mole species protected?
No. All three native Washington mole species can be legally controlled on private property under Washington State law. This is distinct from the **Mazama pocket gopher** (*Thomomys mazama*) — a small rodent, not a mole — which has federally threatened subspecies in parts of Thurston and Pierce Counties. Those are gophers, not moles, and they are protected. If you're unsure whether you have moles or protected gophers, see [Mole vs Vole vs Gopher](/blog/mole-vs-vole-vs-gopher/) for the identification rules.

### Are there gophers and voles in Washington too?
Yes. Voles are small rodents that eat grass, roots, and plant material above and below ground. Gophers are larger rodents that tunnel and eat roots. Both can be confused with moles, but they create different damage patterns and require different control approaches. Moles are insectivores — they eat worms and grubs, not plants.

### Can I tell which species is in my yard from the damage?
For most residential situations, the answer is yes — but you're really telling Townsend's from "anything else." Large clustered volcano mounds on an open lawn are Townsend's every time. Smaller, flatter mounds in a brushier or wooded-edge area might be Pacific Coast. Very small flat mounds near a ravine or stream bank could be Shrew mole. A professional inspection confirms the pattern quickly.

### Do different species need different control methods?
In practice, no. The effective tools and techniques work across Townsend's and Pacific Coast (the two species you'll realistically encounter on residential property). What matters is reading the tunnel system correctly — active runs versus abandoned runs, deep highways versus surface feeding tunnels — and placing equipment where the mole actually travels. Got Moles handles both species with the same proven approach.

### How do I know it's a mole and not something else?
If you're seeing raised ridges across the lawn and volcano-shaped mounds of fresh soil with no visible entrance hole, that's a mole. If you're seeing plant damage at the root, mounds with visible plugs or crescent shapes, or surface paths through grass, that's likely voles or gophers.
