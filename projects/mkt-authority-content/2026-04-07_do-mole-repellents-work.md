# Do Mole Repellents Work? Sonic Stakes, Castor Oil, and Plants Reviewed

**Author:** Spencer Hill, Got Moles
**Last Updated:** {publish date}

---

Most mole repellents don't work. Sonic stakes, castor oil sprays, vibration devices, and "mole-repelling" plants all have one thing in common: no consistent scientific evidence that they remove moles from your yard. Some may temporarily push moles to a different part of your property, but that's not the same as solving the problem. The Washington State University Extension Service has noted that castor oil repellents are "not consistently effective," and independent research backs that up across every repellent category. If you're dealing with active mole damage, physical trapping by a specialist remains the only method with reliable, repeatable results.

Below, we review every major repellent type, the evidence behind each one, and what actually works.

---

## Why Mole Repellents Are So Popular

It makes sense that people try repellents first. They're cheaper than professional service. They're available at every hardware store. And the logic sounds reasonable: make your yard unpleasant for moles, and they'll go somewhere else.

The problem is that moles don't behave the way the packaging suggests. A single mole tunnels up to 18 feet per hour. They maintain networks of tunnels across your entire property. And they're adapted to live underground in conditions most creatures would avoid — including waterlogged soil, vibrating ground, and strong-smelling substances.

Moles have survived 50 million years of evolution. A $30 gadget from Amazon isn't going to change that.

Let's go through each repellent type.

---

## Sonic and Vibration Mole Repellers

**Verdict: Doesn't Work**

Sonic mole repellers are battery-powered or solar-powered stakes you push into the ground. They emit vibrations or ultrasonic pulses designed to irritate moles and drive them away. You'll find hundreds of them on Amazon with mixed reviews and bold claims.

The science doesn't support them. No peer-reviewed study has demonstrated that sonic or vibration devices effectively repel moles. The University of Nebraska-Lincoln Extension and multiple state wildlife agencies have concluded there is no evidence these devices work.

Why moles don't care about your sonic stakes:

1. **Moles live in a world of vibration.** Their entire existence is underground, surrounded by shifting soil, root growth, rain impact, foot traffic, and earthworm movement. Adding one more vibration source doesn't register as a threat.
2. **Moles adapt quickly.** Even if a vibration initially startles a mole, habituation sets in fast. The mole learns the vibration isn't dangerous and returns to normal behavior — often within days.
3. **Coverage is limited.** A single stake covers a small radius. A mole that tunnels 18 feet per hour can work around a few vibration sources without effort.
4. **No directional control.** Even if the device did bother a mole, there's no guarantee it pushes the mole off your property rather than to your garden beds or another part of the lawn.

If you've bought sonic stakes and seen a temporary reduction in activity near the stake, that mole probably just shifted to a different tunnel in a different part of your yard. It didn't leave.

## Castor Oil Repellents

**Verdict: Limited — But Not the Way You'd Hope**

Castor oil-based repellents are the most widely recommended DIY mole treatment. Products like MoleMax and Liquid Fence use castor oil as the active ingredient. You mix them with water and spray them on your lawn, or apply granular versions with a spreader.

The theory: castor oil coats earthworms and grubs, making them taste bad to moles. The mole eats a few unpleasant-tasting worms, decides the food supply is ruined, and moves on.

What actually happens: the Washington State University Extension Service has found castor oil repellents to be "not consistently effective." Some studies show temporary displacement of moles from treated areas, but displacement isn't removal.

The problems with castor oil repellents:

- **Moles often move to untreated parts of the same property.** You spray the front lawn, the moles show up in the backyard. You spray the backyard, they circle back to the front. You haven't solved anything — you've just relocated the damage.
- **Rain washes it away.** In Western Washington, where annual rainfall ranges from 35 to 60 inches depending on location, castor oil treatments don't last. You'd need to reapply after every significant rain event.
- **Incomplete coverage misses deep tunnels.** Moles dig at multiple depths. Surface application of castor oil doesn't reach deeper feeding tunnels where moles spend much of their time.
- **Earthworms are the primary food source, not grubs.** Earthworms make up 55-93% of a mole's diet. Even if castor oil affected grub taste, the mole still has an abundant earthworm supply to feed on. Healthy soil — the kind you want for a nice lawn — is full of earthworms. You can't make all of them taste bad.

If you've tried castor oil and seen some improvement, understand what actually happened: the mole moved. It didn't leave your property. It found food in a part of the yard you didn't treat.

## Plants That Allegedly Repel Moles

**Verdict: Limited Evidence — Don't Count on Them**

Several plants are sold and recommended as mole repellents. The idea is that their roots, bulbs, or compounds irritate moles and create underground "barriers" they won't cross.

The claims versus the reality:

### Do Daffodils Repel Moles?

Daffodil bulbs contain lycorine, a toxic alkaloid. Moles and other burrowing animals won't eat them, and some gardeners report fewer tunnels near daffodil plantings.

The reality: moles don't eat bulbs. They eat earthworms and insects. A mole won't eat your daffodil bulbs, but it will happily tunnel right past them to get to the earthworms living in the same soil. Daffodils protect themselves from being eaten — they don't create a mole barrier.

### Do Marigolds Repel Moles?

Marigolds produce alpha-terthienyl, a compound toxic to certain soil organisms. The claim is that marigolds repel moles through their root chemistry.

The evidence is thin. Marigolds may deter some soil-dwelling insects, but there's no scientific study demonstrating they repel moles. Moles tunnel well below the root depth of annual marigolds. The two barely interact underground.

### Other "Mole-Repelling" Plants

| Plant | Claimed Repellent | Evidence | Reality |
|-------|------------------|----------|---------|
| Daffodils | Lycorine in bulbs | Bulbs are avoided | Moles tunnel past them — they don't eat bulbs anyway |
| Marigolds | Alpha-terthienyl | No direct mole studies | Root depth too shallow to affect mole tunnels |
| Alliums (garlic, onion family) | Strong scent | Anecdotal only | Scent dissipates in soil; no barrier effect demonstrated |

| Euphorbia (mole spurge) | Milky latex sap | Commonly recommended in garden forums | No scientific evidence it repels moles from an area |

The bottom line on plants: no plant has been demonstrated in controlled studies to repel moles from a property. Some of these plants have compounds that deter insects or animals from eating them specifically, but that's self-protection — not area repellency. A mole tunneling 18 feet per hour doesn't stop because it passes a daffodil.

## Flooding and Vibration DIY Methods

**Verdict: Doesn't Work**

Two other common DIY attempts come up all the time:

**Flooding tunnels with a garden hose.** Moles can survive in waterlogged tunnels. Their tunnel systems include drainage channels, and moles are adapted to move through saturated soil. Flooding may temporarily push a mole to higher ground, but it won't kill or remove it. You'll also damage your lawn in the process.

**DIY vibration methods (pinwheels, buried bottles, windmills).** Same principle as sonic stakes, same result. Moles habituate to vibrations quickly. A pinwheel stuck in a molehill is not a pest control strategy.

## Eliminating Grubs to Starve Moles

**Verdict: Doesn't Work**

This myth is everywhere: "Kill the grubs and the moles will leave because they have nothing to eat."

It sounds logical but gets the diet wrong. Moles eat earthworms — not grubs — as their primary food source. Research consistently shows earthworms make up 55-93% of a mole's diet. Grubs, beetles, and other soil insects are a supplement, not the main course.

Applying grub killer to your lawn:
- Removes a small fraction of the mole's food supply
- Does nothing about the earthworm population the mole actually depends on
- May damage beneficial soil organisms that keep your lawn healthy
- Won't cause a mole to leave — the buffet is still open

A healthy lawn with good soil has plenty of earthworms. That's the same lawn that attracts moles. You can't eliminate the food source without destroying the thing you're trying to protect.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do sonic mole repellers actually work?

No. There is no peer-reviewed scientific evidence that sonic or vibration-based devices repel moles. Moles live in an environment full of vibrations and habituate to new ones quickly. Multiple university extension services, including those at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln and Washington State University, have found no evidence supporting these devices.

### Does castor oil get rid of moles permanently?

No. Castor oil may temporarily push moles to untreated areas of your property, but it does not remove them. The Washington State University Extension Service has described castor oil repellents as "not consistently effective." In Western Washington's rainy climate, applications wash away quickly and require constant reapplication.

### Do marigolds or daffodils keep moles away?

There is no scientific evidence that planting marigolds or daffodils repels moles from an area. Daffodil bulbs contain lycorine, which prevents animals from eating the bulbs themselves, but moles don't eat bulbs — they eat earthworms. Marigold roots are too shallow to interact with mole tunnels. Neither plant creates an underground barrier.

### Why doesn't killing grubs get rid of moles?

Because grubs are not the primary food source. Earthworms make up 55-93% of a mole's diet. Eliminating grubs removes a small supplement while leaving the main food supply — earthworms — completely intact. A healthy lawn full of earthworms will continue to attract moles regardless of grub treatment.

### What is the most effective way to get rid of moles?

Professional physical trapping is the only method with consistent, documented results. It directly removes the mole rather than trying to make the environment uncomfortable.
