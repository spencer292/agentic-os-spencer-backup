In 2026, most US solo house cleaners charge $40–75 per hour, and two-person teams bill $50–90 per hour combined, varying by market. But experienced operators don't quote hourly — they quote flat rates built on square footage and clean type: roughly $160–230 for a standard clean of a 2,000 sq ft home, $320–470 for a deep clean, more for move-outs. This guide shows you the production-rate math behind those numbers so you can price any house in your market.

## Should you charge hourly or flat-rate for house cleaning?

Flat-rate, almost every time — because hourly pricing punishes you for getting better at your job. Clean a house in three hours as a rookie, get faster, do it in two, and hourly billing just cut your revenue by a third for the identical result. The client didn't get a worse clean. You got a worse paycheck.

Flat-rate flips that. You quote the house, not the clock. As your speed improves, your effective hourly rate climbs, and the client's price stays predictable — which they prefer anyway. Nobody likes watching a meter run in their own kitchen.

The catch: flat-rate pricing only works when you can predict how long a house takes. That's what production rates are for.

## What's the production-rate math behind a cleaning quote?

A production rate is the square footage one cleaner can cover in an hour, and it's the engine of every accurate flat-rate quote. Reasonable planning numbers for a typical home:

- **Standard/recurring clean:** ~1,000 sq ft per hour per cleaner
- **Deep clean:** ~500 sq ft per hour per cleaner
- **Move-out clean:** ~400 sq ft per hour per cleaner

The math is three steps. Take a 2,000 sq ft deep clean, solo:

1. **Labor hours:** 2,000 ÷ 500 = 4 hours
2. **Labor cost at your target rate:** 4 × $70 = $280 (using a $70/hr target — pick yours from your market's $40–90 range)
3. **Quote:** $280, sanity-checked against supplies, drive time, and your minimum (more on that below). Round to something clean: $285 or $295.

Adjust the base rate for reality: heavy pet hair, hoarding-adjacent clutter, first-time cleans of neglected homes, or lots of stairs and knickknack shelves all slow you down 20–40%. This is why a proper [intake form](/guides/cleaning-business-client-intake-form/) matters before you ever quote — you can't price what you didn't ask about.

Track your actual times per job for a month and your production rates become *your* numbers instead of industry rules of thumb. That's the whole trick. ## How much should you charge by home size and clean type?

Here's a working 2026 flat-rate table for US suburban markets, built from the production rates above. These are ranges, not gospel — a dense coastal metro sits at or above the top, a small rural market near the bottom. Varies by market; check yours against local quotes.

| Home size | Standard clean | Deep clean | Move-out clean |
|---|---|---|---|
| ~1,000 sq ft | $100–150 | $180–280 | $220–330 |
| ~1,500 sq ft | $130–190 | $250–370 | $300–450 |
| ~2,000 sq ft | $160–230 | $320–470 | $380–570 |
| ~2,500 sq ft | $190–270 | $390–560 | $460–680 |
| ~3,000 sq ft | $220–320 | $450–650 | $540–800 |

Two notes on reading this table. First, deep cleans run roughly double a standard clean — because at 500 sq ft/hour, they literally take double the time. Clients think of "deep clean" as a fancier standard clean; you should think of it as twice the labor. Price it that way and stop apologizing for the number.

Second, these are base prices before add-ons: inside the oven, inside the fridge, interior windows, baseboard detail. A well-built add-on menu is the difference between a $160 ticket and a $215 one — that's its own topic, covered in our [price list template guide](/guides/cleaning-service-price-list-template/).

## Why do cleaners give frequency discounts — and how big should they be?

Frequency discounts aren't generosity; they're route-density economics. A weekly client at $140 beats a one-time client at $180, and it's not close. Here's why.

Your real costs per job include drive time, quoting, scheduling back-and-forth, and the re-learning cost of an unfamiliar house. A weekly client amortizes all of that across 52 visits. Their house never gets truly dirty, so each visit is faster than the production-rate math predicts. And they anchor a route day — three recurring homes in one neighborhood means you're cleaning while your competitors are driving. I grew a route-based service company to 5,000 clients on exactly this principle: recurring, geographically dense work compounds; one-off jobs just fill a calendar.

So discount recurring frequency off your one-time price:

- **Weekly:** 20–25% off
- **Biweekly:** 10–15% off
- **Monthly:** 0–10% off (monthly homes get dirty again — discount lightly)

And charge a **first-clean premium** for new recurring clients — the first visit is always closer to a deep clean. Quote it as "first clean $X, then $Y per visit." Clients rarely push back once you explain the first visit resets the house to maintainable.

## What is a minimum job floor and why do you need one?

A minimum job floor is the price below which you decline the work — commonly $100–150 in 2026 suburban markets — because small jobs carry the same fixed costs as big ones. The 600 sq ft apartment "quick clean" still costs you a drive, a setup, a teardown, and a slot on your schedule that could have held a $200 job. At $60, you lost money doing good work.

Set your floor by adding your drive-and-admin cost to an hour of your target rate, then round up. If a job prices below the floor, quote the floor. Some clients walk; the ones who stay are profitable. Both outcomes beat the alternative.

## What are the most common underpricing mistakes?

Run your current pricing against this checklist — most new cleaners fail at least three:

- **Copying the lowest local competitor**, who is usually uninsured, untaxed, and quietly going out of business
- **Quoting hourly and eating your own efficiency gains** as you get faster
- **Pricing deep cleans like standard cleans** instead of at ~2x the labor
- **No first-clean premium**, so every new recurring client starts as a loss
- **No minimum job floor**, filling the schedule with $60 jobs that cost $70 to deliver
- **Ignoring drive time**, which turns a "profitable" far-away job into minimum wage
- **Forgetting non-labor costs** — supplies, insurance, self-employment tax, the 15% of revenue that was never really yours
- **Never raising prices** on old clients, until your oldest clients are your least profitable

That last one deserves a sentence: raise prices on existing clients once a year, with 30 days' written notice, and make sure your [service agreement](/guides/cleaning-business-contract-template/) reserves the right. A 5–8% annual increase loses almost no one and compounds year after year.

## FAQ

**How much should I charge for house cleaning as a solo cleaner in 2026?**
Target $40–75/hour depending on your market, but quote flat rates built on production math: roughly $160–230 for a standard 2,000 sq ft clean, about double that for a deep clean. Varies by market — check local quotes to place yourself in the range.

**Should I charge more for the first cleaning?**
Yes. A first clean is closer to a deep clean than a maintenance visit — quote it at 1.5–2x the recurring price and tell clients why. It resets the house; the recurring price maintains it.

**Do I charge extra for pets?**
For shedding pets, yes — pet hair reliably adds 15–30 minutes to a clean. Either build a flat pet fee ($10–25/visit) into your quote or fold it into a slightly higher base. Ask on intake so it's never a surprise.

**Are my prices supposed to include supplies?**
Usually yes for residential — clients expect you to bring everything, and supplies typically run a small percentage of revenue. Price them in rather than line-iteming them. Commercial accounts sometimes split consumables out separately.

**How do I raise prices without losing clients?**
Annually, in writing, 30 days ahead, with no over-explaining: thank them, state the new price and date, reaffirm the service. A one-page price-increase letter (there's one in our kit) does the job. Expect to lose almost nobody worth keeping.
