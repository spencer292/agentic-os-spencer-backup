A commercial cleaning contract template needs six things a residential agreement doesn't: a task/frequency schedule (usually as an exhibit), a fixed term with renewal terms, Net-15 or Net-30 invoicing, a certificate of insurance requirement, after-hours access protocols, and a 30-day written termination notice with a cure period. Skip any one of these and you'll be renegotiating mid-contract with a property manager who does this for a living.

Quick disclaimer before we start, and it's not optional: templates are starting points, not legal advice. Contract law varies by state and county — have a local attorney review anything before a client signs it.

## Why is a commercial cleaning contract different from a handshake deal?

Because the person signing it isn't the person who'll miss you if you disappear. A homeowner deals with their cleaner directly. A commercial account routes through a property manager, office manager, or facilities director who manages a stack of vendors and reports up to someone else. They need paperwork that survives a staff turnover, an audit, or a "why are we paying this vendor" question from finance.

I ran a route-based service company to 5,000 clients, and the commercial accounts that stuck around were never the ones with the friendliest handshake — they were the ones with a contract clean enough that nobody had to think about us between walkthroughs. That's the whole job of this document: make you low-maintenance on paper so you can be flexible in person.

## What sections does a commercial cleaning contract need?

Six sections carry the weight. Everything past this is negotiation detail specific to the account.

**1. Task and frequency schedule.** Don't bury cleaning tasks in prose. Attach them as an exhibit, broken out by area (lobby, restrooms, break rooms, offices) and frequency (nightly, weekly, monthly). This is what you'll actually get graded against, so make it specific: "vacuum high-traffic carpet nightly, edge-clean weekly" beats "clean carpets as needed."

**2. Term and renewal.** Commercial deals run 6-12 months fixed, with either auto-renewal or a renewal-notice window. A fixed term protects you from a client walking after one bad week, and it gives you a real number to plan your route around.

**3. Payment terms.** Net-15 or Net-30 invoicing is standard for commercial. Expect to bill after the work, not before. Build in a late-payment interest rate (a common range is 1-1.5% per month) so slow-pay accounts have a reason to stop being slow.

**4. Certificate of insurance (COI).** Most commercial clients require proof of general liability insurance before they'll sign, often naming them as an additional insured. Have your insurer generate this ahead of the walkthrough. A delayed COI kills deals that were otherwise closed.

**5. Access and security protocols.** Alarm codes, badge access, after-hours entry, and who to call if something's wrong at 11 p.m. Commercial buildings have security systems residential homes don't, and this section is where you write down exactly how your crew gets in and out without tripping one.

**6. Termination and cure period.** A 30-day written notice to terminate, with a cure period (commonly 10-15 days) that lets you fix a documented problem before the account walks. This protects both sides — the client isn't stuck with bad service, and you aren't blindsided by a cancellation over one missed trash can.

## How does pricing work in a commercial cleaning contract?

Unlike a flat per-visit residential rate, commercial pricing is usually quoted as a monthly or per-visit rate tied to square footage and frequency, with an annual escalator or renegotiation clause built in so you're not locked at 2026 rates through 2028.

| Element | Residential | Commercial |
|---|---|---|
| Pricing basis | Flat rate per visit | Per square foot or monthly, tied to frequency |
| Payment timing | Due at service, card on file | Net-15/Net-30 invoicing |
| Price changes | Price-increase letter, 30 days' notice | Annual escalator or renegotiation clause |
| Term | Ongoing, cancel with notice | Fixed 6-12 month term |
| Supplies | You bring everything | Often negotiated — consumables billed separately |
| Insurance | Mention you're insured | COI required, client often named as additional insured |

If you're pricing a bid, [how much to charge for house cleaning](/guides/how-much-to-charge-for-house-cleaning/) covers the underlying rate math. The commercial version just applies it to square footage and a task schedule instead of a per-visit flat fee.

## What should you not put in a commercial cleaning contract?

Three things that read fine in a residential agreement but cost you credibility in a commercial one:

- **Vague scope language.** "Standard office cleaning" means nothing to a facilities director evaluating three bids. Attach the task/frequency exhibit or you'll lose the comparison on paper before the walkthrough even happens.
- **Personal or informal tone.** Commercial buyers are reading this next to two competitors' contracts. Match the register — plain and direct, not chatty.
- **Missing insurance section, even if you're insured.** "We're insured" without a COI requirement written in is an assumption, not a term. Property managers file COIs; they don't take your word for it.

## Do you need a separate contract for every commercial client?

Yes — even accounts that feel like a formality deserve their own signed agreement with the specific site's task schedule and rate attached. A generic contract with the building name swapped out is easy for an experienced facilities manager to spot, and it signals you're running this as a side gig rather than a real vendor relationship. Pair it with a proper [client intake form](/guides/cleaning-business-client-intake-form/) so the site-specific details (access codes, key contacts, special-handling areas) live somewhere other than someone's memory.

## FAQ

**Does a commercial cleaning contract need to be notarized?**
No, standard commercial service contracts don't require notarization in most states. A dated signature (wet or electronic) from an authorized signer is typically sufficient. Requirements vary by state and by the client's own procurement policy — verify locally.

**What if the property manager sends their own contract instead of using mine?**
Common on larger accounts. Read it closely for scope, payment timing, and liability language before signing — their template is written to protect them, not you. It's reasonable to redline a few terms (payment timing, liability caps) rather than accept it as-is.

**How much should the annual price escalator be?**
There's no fixed number. Many operators build in a clause tied to a fixed percentage (commonly 3-5%) or an index like CPI, reviewed annually. The point is having a mechanism in writing, not a specific figure.

**Do I need a different insurance amount for commercial accounts?**
Often yes — commercial clients, especially larger buildings, sometimes require higher liability limits than a typical residential job. Check with your insurer before bidding a larger account so your quote reflects the real premium.

**Can I use the same cleaning checklists for commercial and residential?**
No — commercial task schedules are organized by area and frequency (lobby nightly, restrooms twice daily, offices weekly) rather than by "clean type" like residential. Build the schedule to match how the space is actually used.
