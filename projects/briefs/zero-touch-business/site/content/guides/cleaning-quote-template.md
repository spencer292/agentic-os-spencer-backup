A cleaning quote template needs five things: the client's name and address, the exact scope of work, a firm price (not a range, that was the intake stage), a start date and frequency, and a clear way to accept. Send it within a few hours of intake, while the conversation is still fresh, and keep it to one page. Below is a full structure you can copy, the difference between a quote, an estimate, and an invoice, and how intake answers turn into the numbers on the page.

## What should a cleaning quote template include?

Five sections, in this order, and nothing that doesn't help the client say yes.

**1. Who and where.** Client name, service address, and the date the quote was prepared. Basic, but a surprising number of quotes skip the address. That's fine for one house and a mess once you're quoting three properties for the same client.

**2. Scope of work.** Exactly what's being cleaned and what isn't. Standard, deep, or move-out clean; which rooms; which add-ons are included versus extra. This is the section that prevents "I thought the fridge was included" three weeks in. Pull this straight from your [price list's add-on menu](/guides/cleaning-service-price-list-template/) so the wording matches what the client already saw.

**3. Price.** One firm number, not the range you quoted verbally at intake. A range belongs on your public price list; a quote is the moment you commit. If recurring, show the per-visit price and the frequency discount separately so the client sees both the rate and the savings.

**4. Timeline.** Proposed start date, and if recurring, the cadence (weekly, biweekly, monthly) and roughly what day of the week. Clients are more likely to book when a date is already on the page. It turns "someday" into a decision between yes and no.

**5. Acceptance.** A signature line, a "reply YES to confirm," or a link to book and pay a deposit. Whatever mechanism you use, make it one step. Quotes that require a phone call to accept lose more deals than quotes with a bad price.

## How is a quote different from an estimate or an invoice?

A quote is a firm commitment before work starts; an estimate is a rough range before work starts; an invoice is the bill after work is done. Cleaning businesses that mix these up create real problems, either quoting like an estimate (too soft, easy to renegotiate down) or invoicing like a quote (surprising the client with add-ons they never agreed to).

| Document | When it's sent | What it commits to | Can price still move? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Estimate | Before or during intake | A rough range | Yes, that's the point |
| Quote | Right after intake, before work | A firm scope and price | No, unless scope changes |
| Contract | Same time as an accepted quote, for recurring work | Ongoing terms, cancellation, access | No, this is the legal layer |
| Invoice | After the job is done | The final bill | Only if you documented a scope change |

The quote is the bridge between "roughly what will this cost" and "here's what you're signing." Treat it as a promise, not a placeholder. If you routinely revise quoted prices upward at the door, clients stop trusting the number on the page, and that reputation travels through reviews faster than almost anything else.

## How do intake answers become the numbers on a quote?

Every line on the quote should trace back to something the client told you at [intake](/guides/cleaning-business-client-intake-form/). Nothing on a good quote is guessed.

- **Home size and condition** → base price tier, pulled from your price list.
- **Pets** → shedding surcharge, if applicable, itemized rather than folded silently into the base price.
- **Priorities** ("the oven, honestly") → add-ons, priced individually so the client sees exactly what they're paying for.
- **Frequency** → recurring discount applied and shown as its own line, not just baked into a lower total.
- **Access notes** → nothing that shows on the quote itself, but worth a one-line confirmation ("we'll use the lockbox code you provided") so the client knows you were listening.

If a quote line doesn't map to an intake answer or the price list, it's a guess, and guesses are where quotes and final invoices start to drift apart.

## Sample cleaning quote template (copy this structure)

Swap in your own numbers and branding; the layout is what matters.

```
[Your Business Name] · Cleaning Quote
Prepared for: [Client Name]
Service Address: [Address]
Date: [Date]

SCOPE OF WORK
Clean type: Standard clean, biweekly
Included: Kitchen, 2 bathrooms, living areas, 3 bedrooms
Tasks: floors, surfaces, bathrooms, dusting, trash
Add-ons: Inside oven ($30, this visit only)

PRICE
Per visit (biweekly): $165
Frequency discount applied: 10% (biweekly)
First clean (includes deep-clean pass): $260

TIMELINE
Proposed start date: [Date]
Ongoing schedule: Every other Tuesday, arrival window 9am–11am

ACCEPTANCE
Reply "YES" to this email to confirm, or call/text [phone] with questions.
A signed service agreement will follow before your first visit.

Quote valid for 7 days.
```

That last line, a validity window, matters more than it looks. Costs and schedules shift, and a quote that's technically open forever eventually gets accepted at a stale price for a job that no longer fits your route.

## How fast should you send a quote after intake?

Same day, ideally within a few hours. Speed is one of the few genuinely free ways to win jobs. A client who called three cleaning companies will often book whoever answers first with a real number, not necessarily the cheapest one. Waiting two days to "batch" your quotes costs you jobs to competitors who didn't wait.

That said, fast doesn't mean careless. A same-day quote with the wrong add-on or a math error undoes the speed advantage the moment the client notices. Keep a template ready, like the one above, so speed and accuracy aren't a trade-off.

## Quote checklist before you hit send

- [ ] Client name and correct service address
- [ ] Scope matches what was discussed at intake, no missing add-ons
- [ ] Price is firm, not a range, and shown per-visit if recurring
- [ ] Frequency discount shown as its own line, not hidden in the total
- [ ] Start date proposed, not left open-ended
- [ ] One clear way to accept (reply, signature, or booking link)
- [ ] Validity window stated (7 days is standard)
- [ ] Note that a signed [service agreement](/guides/cleaning-business-contract-template/) follows before the first visit

## FAQ

**Should a quote include a price range or a firm number?**
Firm number. Ranges belong on your public price list and in the intake conversation; once you've seen the intake answers, commit to a price. If the home turns out different from what was described, that's a scope conversation at the walkthrough, not a reason to quote soft.

**Do I need a separate contract if I already sent a quote?**
Yes, for recurring work. The quote covers scope and price; the [contract](/guides/cleaning-business-contract-template/) covers cancellation terms, access, liability, and what happens if either side wants out. Send the contract as soon as the quote is accepted.

**What if the client wants to negotiate the quoted price?**
Negotiate scope, not just price. Drop an add-on or extend the frequency before cutting the base rate. A lower price for less work protects your margin better than the same work for less money.

**Should quotes expire?**
Yes, 7 days is typical for residential. Your costs, schedule, and route density all shift; an open-ended quote accepted a month later may no longer be a job you actually want at that price.

**Can I send quotes by text instead of email?**
For simple, small jobs, yes, but keep the same five sections even in a text, and follow up with a written copy (email or PDF) before the first visit so there's a record both sides can point to.
