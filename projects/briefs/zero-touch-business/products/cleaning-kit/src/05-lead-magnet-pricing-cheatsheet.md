# Service Business Pricing Cheat Sheet

*How to price local service work so you actually get paid for your time — cleaning, lawn care, windows, pest, junk removal, any trade where you drive to the job.*

---

## The only pricing formula you need

**Price = (Estimated hours × Target hourly rate), rounded UP to the nearest $5 or $10. Quoted flat.**

Three rules inside that one line:

1. **Target hourly rate is revenue per labor hour, not a wage.** It has to cover your pay, taxes, insurance, supplies, the vehicle, and the hours nobody pays for — quoting, driving, invoicing. For most solo operators in 2026 US suburban markets, that number lands somewhere between $50 and $80. If your math says $35, you don't have a business, you have a strenuous hobby.
2. **Estimate hours honestly, then time yourself.** Your first ten jobs are the tuition. Log actual time, fix your estimates, move on.
3. **Quote flat, never hourly.** Customers comparison-shop hourly rates and punish honesty. A flat price gets judged on one thing: is the job worth it to them. When you get faster, a flat rate means you earn more per hour — hourly pricing means you earn less for improving. Read that twice.

## The minimum-job floor

Set the smallest number you will start a vehicle for. Every job carries fixed costs the customer never sees — the drive, the load-out, the setup, the invoice. A "quick $60 job" 25 minutes away is a $28-an-hour job wearing a disguise.

Pick your floor — for most solo service operators it's somewhere in the **$120–$150** range — and make it a rule, not a feeling. Anything below the floor gets quoted **at** the floor. Some callers say no. That's the floor working. The customers who balk at a fair minimum are the same ones who'll balk at everything else, forever.

## The route-density rule of thumb

**Clustered jobs pay more than well-priced jobs.** Not on paper — in your actual bank account.

Two jobs at $150 that are 10 minutes apart beat two jobs at $170 that are 40 minutes apart, because you sell hours and drive time is an hour nobody buys. Rule of thumb: **every stop should be within about 10–15 minutes of another stop on the same day.** Build your schedule by zone — north side Tuesday, south side Thursday — and give your best discounts to customers who fit your route, not customers who haggle hardest. A recurring-client discount isn't generosity; it's you paying for density, and density is the cheapest raise you will ever get.

## Three pricing mistakes new operators make

**1. Copying competitors' prices.** You don't know their costs, their speed, or whether they're profitable — plenty aren't, and they're quoting their way out of business. Pricing off a struggling competitor imports their mistakes. Build your price from your hours and your target rate, then let the market answer.

**2. Pricing to win every job.** If nobody ever says "that's too much," you're the cheapest bid in town, and the cheapest bid attracts the worst customers — slow payers, scope-creepers, tip-of-the-iceberg jobs. A healthy close rate has some no's in it. Aim to win most, not all.

**3. Never raising prices on old clients.** Your first customers got your learning-curve rate. Three years later, half your roster can be paying 2022 prices for 2026 costs. A small annual increase — announced plainly, 30 days ahead — keeps you profitable and almost nobody leaves. The ones who do were your worst-ranked hours anyway.
