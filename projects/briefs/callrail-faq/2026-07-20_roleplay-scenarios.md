# Got Moles Phone Role-Play Pack

Fifteen scenarios built from real recorded calls (April–July 2026). Every opening line is something an actual caller said. Use this pack two ways:

- **Live with Spencer:** Spencer plays the caller, Muhammad answers as Got Moles. Run 2–3 scenarios per session, debrief after each.
- **Solo with Claude:** say **"phone role-play"** in a Claude session — Claude plays the caller, stays in character, then scores the call and tracks progress.

**Facilitator rules (Spencer):** don't rescue him mid-call — let a bad answer play out, the debrief is where teaching happens. Stay in character, including the awkward pauses and repeated questions real callers ask. One curveball per rep at first; stack them as he improves. Pass = he'd have won or correctly handled the caller for real.

**Scoring every rep (100 pts):** Accuracy on pricing/policy 30 · Script arc & qualifying questions 20 · Education & objection handling 20 · Info capture (name, cell, email, address, "how did you find us?") 15 · Tone 15. Passing = 80+.

## Tier 1 — Bread and butter (master these first)

### 1. The price shopper
**Caller:** Ron, Normandy Park. Retired, direct, zero small talk.
**Opens with:** *"I'm trying to get a quote — like, roughly, what is your pricing on getting rid of a mole?"*
**Wants:** a number, fast. Resists the discovery questions.
**Curveball:** "Just give me the number, I don't need the sales pitch."
**Pass:** gives both options cleanly anyway, gets at least location + property size + mole history in before quoting, doesn't chase when the caller says "I'll call around" — holds price, leaves the door open warmly.
**Common mistake:** quoting $450 with no context so it sounds expensive, or discounting unprompted to save the sale.

### 2. Dog, kids, and traps
**Caller:** Rachel, Puyallup. Warm but anxious. Two kids (8, 10) and a golden retriever.
**Opens with:** *"We do have a dog — do I need to worry about these traps with my dog?"*
**Wants:** safety reassurance more than mole facts.
**Curveball:** "How big are the traps? I don't want the animals getting their foot chopped off." Then: "What do you do with the moles when you catch them?"
**Pass:** covered/staked/bucket-over-trap explanation, "tell the kids don't touch," chemical-free framing, and the disposal answer — **double-bagged into YOUR garbage can; we take it with us only on request.** Never gets graphic.
**Common mistake:** dodging the death question. Callers respect matter-of-fact honesty.

### 3. "Is that per mole?"
**Caller:** Anne, Federal Way. Careful with money, re-asks the same question different ways.
**Opens with:** *"It says if we catch them, 150 to start, 450 max — is that per mole?"*
**Wants:** to not get surprise-billed.
**Curveball:** "If you catch two moles it's a $300 balance. If you catch five moles it's a $300 balance?" (asks literally this, twice)
**Pass:** flat-rate explanation lands: *"From one to a million moles, the price doesn't go up or down"* — plus the trust angle (per-mole trappers have an incentive to inflate the count) and the guarantee ($150 is all you pay if nothing's caught).
**Common mistake:** explaining it once and moving on. This caller needs it confirmed two or three times — patience IS the skill.

### 4. Voles, not moles
**Caller:** Bill, Maple Valley. Chatty, unsure what he's got.
**Opens with:** *"I'm pretty sure they're voles. Y'all trap those too?"*
**Wants:** any help at all.
**Curveball:** describes "a hundred tiny one-inch holes in the mulch" — definitely voles. Then: "So you don't know anybody who does voles?"
**Pass:** teaches the ID rule (**mounds or raised tunnels = moles; small open holes and grass streaks = voles**), offers photo ID by text to the business cell (NOT the ad tracking number), and refers out honestly (Trapline / traplineproducts.com for DIY) instead of stretching for the sale.
**Common mistake:** booking a visit "just in case" — an honest no earns referrals; a wasted visit earns a refund.

### 5. "What time will you be here?"
**Caller:** Lynn, existing customer, Auburn. Friendly but needs to leave at 3pm.
**Opens with:** *"Just trying to see if you have an ETA today."*
**Wants:** an exact time you cannot give.
**Curveball:** "Can you just tell him to come before noon?" Then: "Do I need to be home?"
**Pass:** never invents a time. Gives the real flow — quote approved → route optimized → **email day-before, text morning-of, text en-route** — and the unlock: nobody needs to be home, just leave the gate open.
**Common mistake:** promising "probably morning" to be nice. That promise becomes tomorrow's angry no-show call.

## Tier 2 — Judgment calls

### 6. The DIY veteran
**Caller:** Gary, Shoreline. Five years of fighting moles himself. Skeptical of everyone.
**Opens with:** *"I've got a T-handled poison injector that doesn't do squat. I spent $20 on those stupid poison worms and now I have MORE mounds."*
**Wants:** to know why you'd succeed where he failed.
**Curveball:** "Why would your traps work when nothing else has?"
**Pass:** validates instead of mocking (*"that stuff doesn't work — this industry is full of gimmicks"*), explains WHY poison fails (moles smell chemicals; they eat live worms — the wagyu-vs-Beyond-Meat line), and pivots to the worm-supply education. Skeptics convert when the explanation makes them feel smart, not dumb.
**Common mistake:** leading with "you wasted your money."

### 7. "They're back!"
**Caller:** Dana, Federal Way. Used Got Moles last year. Cheerful but exasperated.
**Opens with:** *"Well, I've got some moles again. I think you helped me last year."*
**Wants:** the same fix, quick.
**Curveball:** "Didn't we already pay you to get rid of them? Why are they back?"
**Pass:** frames return as expected, not failure — territorial moles, vacant tunnel network, *"not if but when."* Offers the returning-customer month ($375) AND makes the annual-plan case ("if you're calling me every year, $100/month makes more sense"). Books fast.
**Common mistake:** getting defensive about the previous job.

### 8. The price-sensitive senior
**Caller:** Colleen, Tacoma. On fixed income, comparing you to Mole Masters. Long calls, lots of life detail.
**Opens with:** *"I cannot afford $125 every month. The other company said $395 for a one-time service."*
**Wants:** to be taken seriously and not upsold.
**Curveball:** "Do you offer any senior discounts?" and later, "I need to think about it and call my daughter."
**Pass:** patience with the tangents, honest comparison without trashing the competitor, uses real discount latitude (senior rate, returning-customer pricing) rather than inventing numbers, and lets her leave without pressure while securing the callback info.
**Common mistake:** rushing her, or promising a discount Spencer hasn't authorized. Know the latitude before the call.

### 9. Contract-wary
**Caller:** Deb, Sammamish. Wants the annual plan but scared of commitment.
**Opens with:** *"What if there was a financial situation where we'd maybe have to cancel? Is that an option?"*
**Wants:** the honest fine print.
**Curveball:** "So I'm just checking — there's no forced yearly contract?" (there is a 12-month commitment — don't fudge it)
**Pass:** states terms plainly: 12-month commitment, auto-renews month-to-month after, early exit = 50% of remainder, billed last day of month, no deposit. Then reframes value: *"$3 a day, whole year."* Offers the Quick Fix bridge ($150 setup credits toward annual) if she's still nervous.
**Common mistake:** mumbling past the cancellation fee. Hiding it loses the trust that closes these calls.

### 10. Out of area
**Caller:** Mike, Everett. Nice guy, real mole problem, wrong county.
**Opens with:** *"I am in Everett, Washington — do you service this area?"*
**Wants:** help; will settle for a direction.
**Curveball:** "Is there any flexibility at all? What if we come mow your lawn for a couple years?" (a real Vashon caller offered this)
**Pass:** clean no with a warm referral — Kirk at Northwest Mole King for north sound, Terry Cotton for Kitsap — and DIY guidance (Trapline traps) where nobody serves them. Caller should hang up liking the company.
**Common mistake:** a bare "no, sorry, bye." Referrals cost nothing and these callers leave five-star reviews anyway.

### 11. The spouse deferral
**Caller:** Priya, Sammamish. Fully sold, but not the decision-maker.
**Opens with:** *"Let me forward this to my husband to see which option to go with."*
**Wants:** an exit without committing.
**Curveball:** if pushed hard, she goes cold and vague.
**Pass:** doesn't push — sends the quote to BOTH spouses (get the second email/phone), sets a concrete follow-up ("I'll check back tomorrow afternoon if I haven't heard"), and plants one sentence for the dinner conversation (flat rate + nothing-caught guarantee).
**Common mistake:** treating it as a rejection and letting the lead die, or closing so hard she stops answering.

## Tier 3 — Hard mode

### 12. The no-show
**Caller:** Richard, existing customer. Second call of the morning. Polite words, hot tone.
**Opens with:** *"Spencer was supposed to come yesterday, but he didn't show up, didn't send me any text. I just don't know what's going on."*
**Wants:** acknowledgment first, a fix second.
**Curveball:** "This is the second time I've called about this today. Is anyone actually going to call me back?"
**Pass:** apologize without excuses, own it on behalf of the company, reschedule to a concrete day RIGHT NOW on the call, and promise (then flag) the proactive-text fix. No blaming the router, the tech, or the system.
**Common mistake:** explaining why it happened before acknowledging it happened.

### 13. The property manager
**Caller:** Janet, The Management Trust (HOA). All business, unfamiliar vocabulary.
**Opens with:** *"I need bids on mole abatement for three townhouse units, and we'll need you set up as a compliant vendor — COI, W-9, the usual."*
**Wants:** paperwork competence and a time window she can give her board.
**Curveball:** "Can you be out there this week? The board meets Thursday."
**Pass:** doesn't fake answers — captures exactly what's needed (units, addresses, compliance docs, deadline, billing contact), sets expectations honestly, and routes to Spencer same-day with a complete note. Knowing what you DON'T handle is the pass condition.
**Common mistake:** quoting residential pricing at a commercial job, or promising vendor paperwork sight-unseen.

### 14. The legality challenge
**Caller:** Tom, Burien. Genuinely curious, slightly confrontational.
**Opens with:** *"Oh, so you're actually allowed to kill them? I didn't think in Washington we were allowed to."*
**Wants:** to check you're legit.
**Curveball:** "I read there was some law about trapping in Washington."
**Pass:** uses Spencer's framing exactly — pest-control trapping is legal; the restrictions people remember concern fur-trapping of furbearers — delivered calm and unrattled (Spencer answers this five times a day). Bridges back to chemical-free, safe-for-pets professional methods. **No claims beyond that framing.**
**Common mistake:** improvising legal commentary or getting defensive. Stick to the script.

### 15. The callback (outbound — hardest rep)
**Setup:** Muhammad calls OUT to a lead the AI intake captured yesterday. The AI logged the address wrong ("3302 Bond Road" — caller said 302, twice, and hung up swearing). The lead has since gotten a competitor quote.
**Caller answers with:** *"Oh — yeah, I called you guys yesterday. Honestly your machine kind of screwed up my address twice, and Mole Masters already called me back."*
**Pass:** opens with the fix ("I've got you at 302 Bond Road — is that right?"), apologizes for the machine briefly without dwelling, leads with what the caller was blocked on (pricing + when we can come), and offers speed as the differentiator. Re-verifies EVERY detail the AI captured.
**Common mistake:** launching into the standard inbound script on an outbound call, or bad-mouthing the competitor.

## Rapid-fire drill (5 minutes, any session)

Fire these back-to-back; each answer in under 20 seconds, accurate:

1. How much does it cost? · 2. Is that per mole? · 3. What if you don't catch anything? · 4. How soon can you come? · 5. What time will you be here? · 6. Do I need to be home? · 7. Is it safe for my dog? · 8. Do you use poison? · 9. Are these moles or voles? · 10. Can I cancel the annual plan? · 11. Do you service [Everett / Vashon / Kenmore]? (three different answers!) · 12. Do moles go away in winter? · 13. How many moles do I have? · 14. What do you do with them after? · 15. Is this even legal in Washington? · 16. Any discounts? · 17. Do you do weekends? · 18. Can someone come look first? · 19. How do I pay? · 20. Why did they come back after you trapped one?

## Progression plan

- **Week 1:** Tier 1 only + rapid-fire daily. Pass all five twice before moving on.
- **Week 2:** Tier 2, one new scenario per day, re-run any Tier 1 that scored under 90.
- **Week 3:** Tier 3 + curveball stacking (two per call) + outbound callback reps.
- **Ongoing:** one random scenario per week, cold. When a real call goes sideways, turn it into scenario #16, #17, ... — this pack should grow.
