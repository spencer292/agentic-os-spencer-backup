## 2. Current Internal Linking Audit — What's Working

### Strengths

**Homepage links well to money pages.** The homepage links directly to all three service pages, the How It Works page, the Service Areas hub, and 12 featured location pages. This is solid. The most authoritative page on the site is distributing equity to the right places.

**Service pages cross-link to each other.** The TMCP page has an "Also Consider" section linking to One-Time Removal and Commercial. This is best practice for keeping users in the consideration funnel.

**Service pages link down to location pages.** Each service page includes a "Service Areas" section with 12 featured location links plus a link back to the Service Areas hub. Good for pushing equity down the hierarchy.

**Location pages link up to all three service pages.** The Sammamish page (representative sample) links to TMCP, One-Time, and Commercial. This creates the proper authority loop where location pages push equity up to money pages.

**Blog posts have some contextual in-content links.** The cornerstone guide "How to Get Rid of Moles in Your Yard" links to supporting articles like "Mole vs Vole vs Gopher," "What Do Moles Eat?" and "Does Grub Control Stop Moles." This is the correct pillar/cluster model.

**FAQ page exists and answers common questions.** This is a good hub for contextual linking opportunities.

**Blog index has cluster filtering.** The blog categories (Mole Control, Biology, Safety, Cost & Value, Seasonal, DIY vs Pro) create topical groupings that support topic cluster architecture.

## 3. Current Internal Linking Audit — What's Missing or Weak

### Critical Gaps

#### Gap 1: Blog Posts Don't Link to Location Pages
None of the blog posts appear to link to specific location pages. This is a significant missed opportunity. Blog content about Western Washington mole behaviour should be linking to the cities where that behaviour is most relevant. For example:

- "What Attracts Moles to Your Yard?" mentions irrigated PNW lawns. It should link to 2–3 high-priority location pages like Sammamish, Bellevue, or Puyallup where irrigated lawns are the norm.
- "When Are Moles Most Active in Washington State?" should link to location pages in river valley communities (Puyallup Valley, Green River corridor) where seasonal activity is heaviest.
- "Why Do Moles Keep Coming Back After Treatment?" should link to location pages near forested parkland (Mill Creek, Ravensdale, Sammamish) where reinvasion is a documented problem.

**Impact:** Location pages are the hardest to rank because they compete in hyper-local SERPs. Contextual links from topically relevant blog content are one of the strongest signals you can send.

#### Gap 2: Location Pages Don't Link to Blog Content
The Sammamish sample page has no links to any blog posts. When the page discusses "Why Moles Thrive in Sammamish" and mentions Alderwood soil, earthworms, and rainfall, it should be linking to "What Attracts Moles to Your Yard?" and "What Do Moles Eat?" and "How Deep Do Moles Dig?"

When the Sammamish FAQ answers "Why do moles keep coming back?" it should link to the dedicated blog post on that exact topic.

**Impact:** This is a two-way authority flow problem. Without blog-to-location and location-to-blog links, these two content types operate as silos rather than reinforcing each other.

#### Gap 3: Location Pages Don't Link to Neighbouring Location Pages
The Sammamish page mentions Klahanie, Sahalee, Pine Lake, and Aldarra — all neighbourhoods — but doesn't link to adjacent city pages like Issaquah, Redmond, or Bellevue. Moles don't respect city boundaries, and neither should the internal linking.

Linking between neighbouring location pages creates a local topical mesh that signals comprehensive geographic coverage to Google and helps users find coverage for their specific area.

#### Gap 4: FAQ Page Answers Don't Link to Relevant Deep Content
The FAQ page has 25+ questions and answers but minimal internal links. Every answer is a contextual linking opportunity:

- "How much does mole removal cost?" → link to the blog post "How Much Does Mole Removal Cost in Washington State?"
- "Is your method safe for pets?" → link to "Is Mole Control Safe for Pets?"
- "What time of year are moles most active?" → link to "When Are Moles Most Active in Washington State?"
- "How long does it take to get rid of moles?" → link to How It Works page
- "What's the difference between one-time removal and TMCP?" → link to both service pages

#### Gap 5: About Page Has Minimal Internal Linking
The About page tells Spencer's story and introduces the team but only links to Reviews and Contact. This page likely earns backlinks (people link to founder stories), so it should pass that equity to money pages. It should link to How It Works, the TMCP page (mentioned in the narrative), and 2–3 location pages around the Buckley/Enumclaw origin story area.

#### Gap 6: Blog Posts Don't Consistently Cross-Link Within Clusters
The cornerstone guide does link to several supporting posts, but the supporting posts themselves appear to have limited cross-linking to each other. For example:

- "Are Moles Blind?" should link to "How Many Eyes Do Moles Have?" and vice versa
- "Do Moles Bite?" should link to "Are Moles Poisonous or Venomous?" and "Do Moles Carry Diseases?"
- "Can Moles Swim?" should link to "How Deep Do Moles Dig?"
- "How Many Babies Do Moles Have?" should link to "How Long Do Moles Live?" and "Why Do Moles Keep Coming Back?"

#### Gap 7: Reviews Page is a Dead End
The Reviews page likely has social proof content but doesn't appear to link back to service pages or location pages. When a review mentions a specific city ("Tacoma, WA"), that's a natural contextual link to the Tacoma location page. When a review praises year-round protection, that's a natural link to the TMCP page.

#### Gap 8: No "Related Articles" or "Further Reading" Sections on Blog Posts
Blog posts end without suggesting next reads. Adding a "Related Articles" section at the bottom of each post (3–4 links to related content) would significantly increase pages-per-session and distribute authority across the blog cluster.

## 4. Recommended Internal Linking Architecture

### The Hub-and-Spoke Model for Got Moles

Based on current best practices, Got Moles should implement a three-tier hub-and-spoke model:

**Tier 1 — Authority Hubs (receive the most internal links)**
- Homepage
- TMCP service page
- One-Time Removal service page
- "How to Get Rid of Moles in Your Yard" (cornerstone blog post)

**Tier 2 — Supporting Hubs (link up to Tier 1, receive links from Tier 3)**
- How It Works
- Service Areas hub page
- Blog cluster pillar posts (one per cluster)
- Commercial Mole Control
- About page
- FAQ page

**Tier 3 — Supporting Content (link up to Tier 1 and Tier 2)**
- All 92+ location pages
- All supporting blog posts
- Reviews page

### Ideal Link Flow

```
Homepage
├── TMCP ←→ One-Time ←→ Commercial (cross-linked)
│   ├── Location Pages (link up to services, across to neighbours)
│   └── Blog Posts (link up to services and relevant locations)
├── How It Works → Services
├── Service Areas Hub → All Location Pages
├── Blog Index → All Posts → Cornerstone → Services
├── FAQ → Blog Posts + Services
├── About → Services + Key Locations
└── Reviews → Services + Locations (via testimonial context)
```

## 6. Anchor Text Strategy

### Rules for Got Moles

1. **Use descriptive, natural anchors.** Not "click here" or "learn more." Instead: "our year-round mole protection programme," "mole control in Sammamish," "why moles keep coming back."

2. **Vary the anchor text.** Don't always use "mole control in [city]" for location page links. Mix it up: "Sammamish homeowners," "properties on the Eastside," "communities like Kirkland and Bellevue."

3. **Match the anchor to the destination page's primary keyword.** The TMCP page targets "year-round mole protection" and "total mole control program" — use anchors in that family. The One-Time page targets "one-time mole removal" and "mole removal cost" — anchor accordingly.

4. **For blog-to-blog links, use the question or topic as the anchor.** "What do moles actually eat" linking to the "What Do Moles Eat?" post. This is natural and descriptive.

5. **For location pages, mix geo+service anchors.** "Professional mole trapping in Tacoma," "Bellevue mole control," "serving the Puyallup Valley."

## 7. Technical Implementation Notes

### Link Placement Priority

Contextual body links carry the most weight. In order of SEO value:

1. **In-content contextual links** (within paragraph text) — highest value
2. **Section-specific navigation** (e.g., "Related Articles," "Nearby Service Areas") — strong value
3. **Sidebar or widget links** — moderate value
4. **Footer/navigation links** — lowest per-link value (but still important for crawlability)

### Target Links Per Page

Based on current best practice guidance of 2–5 contextual links per 1,000 words:

| Page Type | Approx. Word Count | Recommended Contextual Links |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| Blog posts (long) | 2,000–3,000 | 6–12 internal links |
| Blog posts (short) | 500–1,000 | 3–5 internal links |
| Location pages | 1,000–1,500 | 5–8 internal links |
| Service pages | 1,500–2,000 | 5–8 internal links |
| FAQ page | 2,000+ | 10–15 internal links |
| About page | 1,500 | 5–8 internal links |
| How It Works | 1,500 | 5–8 internal links |

### Crawl Depth Rule

Every page should be reachable within 3 clicks from the homepage. Current structure largely achieves this:

- Homepage → Service page (1 click)
- Homepage → Service Areas → Location page (2 clicks)
- Homepage → Blog Index → Blog post (2 clicks)
- Homepage → FAQ (1 click)

No orphan pages detected in the current structure. The footer navigation and Service Areas hub page keep all location pages within 2 clicks.

## 9. Ongoing Maintenance Checklist

For every **new blog post published:**
- Link from the post to the cornerstone guide
- Link from the post to 1–2 relevant service pages
- Link from the post to 2–3 relevant location pages
- Link from the post to 2–3 related blog posts
- Add a link TO the new post from the cornerstone guide (if relevant)
- Add a link TO the new post from 2–3 existing related blog posts
- Add a link TO the new post from the FAQ page (if it answers a listed question)

For every **new location page published:**
- Link to all 3 service pages
- Link to the Service Areas hub
- Link to 3–5 relevant blog posts
- Link to 3–5 neighbouring location pages
- Add a link TO the new page from the Service Areas hub
- Add a link TO the new page from relevant blog posts
- Add a link TO the new page from neighbouring location pages

**Quarterly audit:**
- Check for orphan pages (pages with zero internal links pointing to them)
- Check for broken internal links
- Review anchor text variety — flag any anchor used more than 5 times
- Verify all pages are within 3 clicks of the homepage
- Review page-per-session and bounce rate by page type to identify weak link paths

## 10. Expected Impact

Based on the research, a well-executed internal linking strategy for a site of this size and structure should deliver:

- **Improved crawl efficiency** — Google discovers and indexes new content faster when the internal link mesh is dense and logical.
- **Higher rankings for location pages** — Location pages receiving contextual links from topically relevant blog content will see ranking improvements for "[city] mole control" queries.
- **Increased time on site** — Users who land on a blog post and find relevant links to service pages and location pages stay longer and convert at higher rates.

Proper internal linking ties that cluster together and signals to Google (and to AI search engines) that got-moles.com is the definitive authority on mole control in Western Washington.
