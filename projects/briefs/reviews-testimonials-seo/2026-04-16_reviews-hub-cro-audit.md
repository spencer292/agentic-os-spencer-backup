## LIFT Model Scores

### Value Proposition: 8/10 (weight: 30%)

**Evidence for:**
- Headline "WHAT OUR CUSTOMERS SAY" is clear and unambiguous. Jennifer knows exactly what page she's on.
- Subheading gives the volume claim (219+) and geographic scope (3 locations) immediately.
- Featured reviews (Section 4) deliver on the promise with specific outcomes: 27 moles, 5 acres, 22 years resolved.
- Expert attribution (Section 8) adds Spencer's credentials. Named founder with veteran status and 15+ years.

**Evidence against:**
- Headline is descriptive, not outcome-oriented. "WHAT OUR CUSTOMERS SAY" is functional. Compare to: "219+ HOMEOWNERS TRUST GOT MOLES TO PROTECT THEIR YARDS." The current headline tells you what the page is. An outcome headline tells you what to believe.
- The subheading carries the real value prop (219+ reviews). On mobile, if the headline wraps to 2 lines, the subheading may push below the fold.

**Recommendation (Test):** A/B test the headline. Keep the functional "WHAT OUR CUSTOMERS SAY" as control. Test an outcome variant like "219+ FIVE-STAR REVIEWS FROM HOMEOWNERS LIKE YOU" or "TRUSTED BY 5,000+ WESTERN WASHINGTON HOMEOWNERS." The subheading can then carry the 3-location detail.

### Relevance: 9/10 (weight: 20%)

**Evidence for:**
- Blueprint maps 6 entry points with distinct emotional states. Each is addressed:
  - Google "Got Moles reviews" lands on exactly what they searched for
  - Homepage "See All 219+" delivers on the promise with 183+ reviews (not 6)
  - City-specific queries hit the filter bar + city GEO paragraphs
  - TMCP validation queries hit the service type filter
- Filter bar (Section 5) lets Jennifer self-select her relevance group (city, service type)
- City GEO paragraphs (Section 6) with named local customers match local intent
- FAQ (Section 9) directly answers the decision-stage queries Jennifer types into Google

**Evidence against:**
- Commercial segment (property managers) has no entry point or filter context. The blueprint is 100% residential. ICP Segment 2 (property manager) arrives with different questions: reliability, contracts, portfolio management. No FAQ addresses commercial concerns.

**Recommendation (Fix now):** Add one FAQ question: "Does Got Moles work with commercial properties and property managers?" Answer: cite commercial review count (6 from text enrichment), annual contracts, multi-site capability. Link to Commercial service page. Low effort, closes the gap for Segment 2.

### Clarity: 9/10 (weight: 20%)

**Evidence for:**
- Section flow follows a natural decision journey: volume claim (hero) > proof of claim (stats + featured) > self-selection (filters) > full proof (grid) > authority (Spencer) > objection handling (FAQ) > convert (CTA)
- Every section has ONE job. No section tries to do two things.
- Progressive disclosure on review cards (truncate + expand) prevents wall-of-text overwhelm
- Filter bar with count badges ("Seattle (96)") gives Jennifer information architecture she can process while scanning on her phone
- FAQ uses accordion pattern. Only one question open at a time. Clean.

**Evidence against:**
- 10 sections is a lot of page. Jennifer is time-poor (ICP: "searching between tasks"). The journey from hero to CTA is long. She may not reach Section 9 (FAQ) or Section 10 (CTA) on a phone.
- Statistics Bar (Section 3) partially duplicates trust strip data (5-star rated, 5,000+ clients). Jennifer sees similar numbers twice in the first two viewports.

**Recommendation (Test):** Consider merging Sections 2 (GEO definition) and 3 (Statistics Bar) into a single "proof summary" section. The GEO paragraph runs as text. The stats run as large numbers below it. Same content, one section. Saves one scroll on mobile. The GEO content stays in the DOM for AI engines regardless.

### Urgency: 6/10 (weight: 10%)

**Evidence for:**
- Internal urgency is well-built: Jennifer's frustration is mirrored in the featured reviews (tried everything, moles keep coming back). The FAQ "What happens if moles come back?" speaks to her fear of repetition.
- The transformation from 6 reviews to 183+ is itself a trust accelerator. The sheer volume creates a "these people figured it out, why haven't I?" pressure.

**Evidence against:**
- No seasonal or temporal urgency. Mole activity is seasonal (spring peak in Western WA). The blueprint has no mention of timing: "Moles are most active in spring. Early treatment prevents lawn damage that costs $X to repair." This is a missed lever.
- No scarcity signal. Not forced scarcity (no countdown timers, that's cheap). Real scarcity: "Spencer's team covers 60+ cities. Spring is our busiest season. Schedule your inspection before the calendar fills."
- The CTA "READY TO SEE RESULTS LIKE THESE?" is aspirational. Not urgent.

**Recommendation (Fix now):** Add a seasonal context line to the GEO definition block or statistics bar: "Moles are most active from March through June in Western Washington. Early intervention prevents the compounding damage that makes removal harder and more expensive." This is factual, not manipulative. It creates real internal urgency.

### Anxiety: 9/10 (weight: 10%)

**Evidence for:**
- The entire page IS anxiety reduction. 183+ reviews with names, cities, ratings. Featured reviews address the #1 objection ("Will this actually work?") with specific outcomes.
- Expert attribution (Section 8) with Spencer's credentials reduces "who is this company?" anxiety.
- FAQ directly handles objections: safety, cost, effectiveness, return prevention.
- Filter bar lets Jennifer see reviews from HER city. Local proof is the strongest anxiety reducer for local services.
- Star ratings visible on every review. Gold stars are an instant trust signal.

**Evidence against:**
- No guarantee mention on the reviews page. The TMCP guarantee ("new activity between visits, we come back at no extra charge") is a powerful anxiety reducer that only appears on the TMCP page. Jennifer reading reviews doesn't know it exists unless she clicks through to TMCP from a FAQ answer. A brief mention in the expert attribution block or near the CTA would reduce friction.

**Recommendation (Fix now):** Add one line to the expert attribution block (Section 8) or the final CTA (Section 10): "Every Total Mole Control Program member is backed by our guarantee: if moles return between visits, we come back at no extra charge." Keep it programme-specific per the guarantee scope rule. Do NOT make it a sitewide claim.

### Distraction: 9/10 (weight: 10%)

**Evidence for:**
- CTA map is clean: hero CTA at top, final CTA at bottom. Everything in between is proof. No competing actions.
- Filter bar is navigation, not distraction. It increases relevance.
- FAQ internal links are contextual (embedded in answers), not visual CTAs that compete.
- Expert attribution secondary link (About page) is a text link, visually subordinate.
- One primary CTA per viewport rule is satisfied across the entire page.

**Evidence against:**
- The filter bar could become a distraction if the city GEO paragraphs (Section 6) are too long or the transition is jarring. If Jennifer is scanning reviews and accidentally taps a filter, the context shift could lose her.
- Load More button in the review grid (Section 7) is a mild distraction. Every time Jennifer taps it, she's not calling. Infinite scroll or auto-load on scroll-near-bottom would keep her in flow state.

**Recommendation (Test):** Replace "Load More" button with auto-load on scroll (IntersectionObserver). Reviews load seamlessly as Jennifer scrolls. She stays in the proof flow without interruption. The button is a speed bump.
