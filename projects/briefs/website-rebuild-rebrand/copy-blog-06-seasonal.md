# When Are Moles Most Active in the Pacific Northwest? A Seasonal Guide

**Author:** Spencer Hill, Got Moles
**Last Updated:** {publish date}

---

## [BLUF Opening]

Moles are active year-round in Washington State — they do not hibernate. However, mole activity is most visible in spring (March-May) and autumn (September-November) when soil moisture is highest and moles dig closer to the surface. In Western Washington's wet climate, mole damage can appear in any month. The Townsend's mole, the dominant species in the Puget Sound region, remains active through winter and can push soil to the surface even during the coldest months.

## Moles Don't Hibernate

This is the single most important fact about mole activity in the Pacific Northwest: moles are active 365 days a year. They don't hibernate. They don't migrate. They don't take winter off.

What changes is how visible their activity is. Moles dig at different depths depending on the season, which affects how much surface damage you see. But underground, they're always at it — eating, tunnelling, expanding their territory no matter the month.

---

## The Seasonal Pattern in Western Washington

### Spring (March-May) — Peak Visibility

Spring is when most homeowners first notice mole damage. Two reasons:

1. **Soil moisture peaks.** Western Washington's wet springs saturate the ground, forcing earthworms toward the surface — and moles follow their food. Surface-level tunnelling produces the mounds and ridges that wreck lawns.

2. **Breeding season.** Moles breed in late winter and early spring. Young moles leave their mother's territory in spring, actively seeking new ground. Your yard that was clear in February can have fresh mole activity by April — not because a mole came back, but because a new one moved in.

Spring is our busiest season at Got Moles. Call volume doubles between March and May.

### Summer (June-August) — Deeper Activity, Less Visible

As the soil dries in summer, moles dig deeper to follow moisture and earthworms. Surface damage often drops, which leads homeowners to think the moles have left. They haven't. They've just moved deeper underground where the soil stays cool and damp.

Irrigated lawns are the exception. If you water your lawn regularly through summer, you're keeping the moist conditions moles prefer right at the surface. Plenty of Sammamish and Bellevue homeowners see summer mole activity precisely because their well-maintained, irrigated lawns create an oasis in otherwise dry conditions.

### Autumn (September-November) — Second Peak

When the autumn rains return, the pattern reverses. Soil moisture goes up, earthworms move toward the surface, and moles follow. Fresh mounds reappear — often in areas that looked clear all summer.

Autumn is also when young moles from the spring breeding cycle are fully grown and staking out their own territories. A second wave of new arrivals that catches plenty of homeowners off guard.

### Winter (December-February) — Still Active, Less Visible

Moles stay active through Western Washington's mild winters. They tunnel below the frost line (which is shallow in the Puget Sound region — rarely deeper than 6 inches) and keep feeding on earthworms. You may see fewer surface mounds, but underground activity doesn't stop.

In particularly mild, wet winters — common in Western Washington — mole damage can be visible year-round with no real break.

## Why This Matters for Mole Control Timing

The best time to deal with a mole problem is as soon as you notice it. Waiting for a "better season" doesn't work because:

- Moles don't leave on their own — they stay in their territory year-round
- Every week of delay means more tunnelling, more damage, and a more established mole
- New moles can move into cleared territory at any time, especially during spring and autumn dispersal

If you're seeing mole damage right now, the moles are active right now. That's true in March and true in December.

## FAQ

**Do moles hibernate in Washington?**
No. Moles are active year-round in Washington State. They may dig deeper during dry summer months and cold winter periods, but they never go dormant. The Townsend's mole remains active in all seasons.

**When is the worst time for moles in Seattle?**
Spring (March-May) and autumn (September-November) produce the most visible mole damage in the Seattle area due to high soil moisture and mole breeding/dispersal cycles. But irrigated lawns can see activity year-round.

**Should I wait until spring to deal with moles?**
No. If moles are active on your property now, the best time to deal with it is now. Waiting lets the mole expand its tunnel network and cause more damage.
