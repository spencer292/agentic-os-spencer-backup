## You Didn't Do Anything Wrong

If your moles came back after treatment, it's not because the treatment failed. It's because mole biology works against one-time fixes.

## Three Reasons Moles Return

### 1. Your Yard Is Still Attractive

The mole didn't pick your yard at random. It showed up because of specific conditions:

- **Moist, loamy soil** — Townsend's moles love the well-watered soil that's common across Western Washington
- **Healthy earthworm populations** — earthworms make up 85%+ of a Townsend's mole's diet. Healthy lawn means healthy earthworms means mole food
- **Loose, diggable ground** — irrigated, aerated, and well-maintained lawns are easier to tunnel through

Removing the mole doesn't change any of that. Your yard is still exactly what the next mole is looking for.

### 2. Territory Gets Filled

Moles are territorial. Each one holds a defined area and defends it from other moles. When a mole is removed, its territory goes vacant — and neighboring moles expand into it, or young moles on the move claim it.

This happens most in spring and autumn, when young moles leave their mother's territory and search for unclaimed ground. A recently cleared property is a prime target.

In areas with high mole density — most of suburban Western Washington qualifies — vacant territory doesn't stay vacant long. Re-invasion can happen in as little as a few weeks.

### 3. Adjacent Properties Are Untreated

Your property doesn't exist on its own. If the lots around you have moles, you've got a constant supply of new arrivals. They tunnel through from neighboring yards, from nearby parks, from green corridors and wooded areas.

This comes up all the time in Sammamish, Bellevue, Kirkland, and other suburbs where homes back onto natural areas. The forest is an unlimited mole reservoir, and your lawn is a more comfortable destination.

## What Doesn't Work Long-Term

### Repeated one-time treatments
Each treatment clears the current moles, but the cycle restarts. If you're paying $290-$450 per treatment and moles return twice a year, you're spending $580-$900 annually — with two separate rounds of damage each time.

### Repellents and deterrents
Castor oil, sonic stakes, and other deterrents don't create a permanent barrier. At best, they redirect mole activity for a while. The moles don't leave your property — they find a different route.

### Grub control
Killing grubs doesn't touch the primary food source. Townsend's moles eat earthworms, not grubs. A yard with zero grubs can still have a thriving mole population.
