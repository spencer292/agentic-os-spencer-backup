## About Moles

**What species of moles live in Washington State?**
Three mole species are found in Washington. The Townsend's mole is the most common in Western Washington and the largest mole in North America, reaching up to 8 inches in length. It's the one responsible for the large mounds that destroy lawns across the Puget Sound region. The Pacific mole (or coast mole) is smaller and sticks to drier, wooded habitats. The shrew mole is the smallest and causes less damage but shows up in forested areas throughout Western Washington.

**Are moles nocturnal?**
Not strictly. They're active in cycles throughout the day and night, alternating between bursts of activity and rest every few hours. Most surface-level tunnelling happens during early morning and late evening when the soil is moist and earthworms are near the surface. You might notice fresh mole mounds at any time of day.

**Do moles hibernate?**
No. Moles are active year-round in Washington State. They don't hibernate, migrate, or go dormant in winter. During colder months, they dig deeper tunnels below the frost line (which is shallow in Western Washington — rarely more than 6 inches) but keep feeding and tunnelling. That's why Got Moles offers year-round protection through the TMCP.

**What do moles eat?**
Moles are insectivores. The Townsend's mole eats primarily earthworms, which make up roughly 85% or more of its diet. They also eat grubs, beetle larvae, and other soil-dwelling insects. This is why grub control products don't fix mole problems — the main food source is earthworms, not grubs. A single Townsend's mole can eat 70-100% of its body weight in earthworms every day.

**How do I know if I have moles or voles?**
Moles create raised ridges (surface tunnels) and volcano-shaped mounds of soil pushed up from deep tunnels. They eat earthworms and insects — they don't eat plants. Voles create small, round holes at the surface (about the size of a golf ball) and eat plant roots, bulbs, and grass. Vole damage includes chewed bark at the base of trees and plants that wilt or die. If you see soil mounds with no visible entry hole, you've likely got moles. If you see small surface holes with plant damage, you've likely got voles.

**Can moles come back after treatment?**
Yes. Moles are territorial, and when one is removed, the empty territory can be claimed by neighboring moles or young moles on the move from other areas. In Western Washington's mole-dense environment, re-invasion is common within 3-12 months after a one-time treatment. That's the main reason Got Moles built the Total Mole Control Program — ongoing monitoring catches new moles before they settle in and cause damage.

## Common Concerns

**Why don't store-bought traps work?**
Effective mole trapping requires precise placement in active tunnels at the right depth and angle. Without experience reading mole behavior — telling active runs from abandoned tunnels, identifying travel direction, avoiding scent contamination — most homeowners put traps where moles aren't actually travelling. Professional mole control exists because the knowledge behind placement matters more than the trap itself.

**Does castor oil repel moles?**
Not reliably. Washington State University's Extension Service says castor oil and similar repellents are "not consistently effective" against moles. In our experience, castor oil might redirect mole activity to a different part of the yard for a while, but it doesn't drive moles off the property. The moles stay — they just dig somewhere else.

**Does grub control reduce moles?**
No. This is one of the most common misconceptions about mole control. Moles do eat grubs, but their main food source is earthworms — typically 85% or more of a Townsend's mole's diet. Getting rid of grubs doesn't meaningfully cut the food supply that draws moles in. A yard with zero grubs can still support a thriving mole population as long as earthworms are present — and in Western Washington's moist soil, earthworms are always present.

**Is it safe to leave moles alone?**
Moles aren't dangerous to people or pets, and they do aerate the soil. But a single Townsend's mole can create over 200 mounds per year, tearing up lawns, garden beds, root systems, and irrigation lines. Mole tunnels also create trip hazards and can undermine walkways and patios. Left alone, the damage usually gets worse — moles don't pack up and leave on their own.

**I don't want to kill the moles. Are there humane options?**
We understand the concern. Relocating moles isn't practical — they don't survive relocation, and Washington law doesn't permit it. The most humane approach is a fast, effective resolution by a trained specialist — minimizing the duration of damage and disruption for everyone involved. Got Moles uses professional trapping methods that resolve the problem quickly and cleanly.
