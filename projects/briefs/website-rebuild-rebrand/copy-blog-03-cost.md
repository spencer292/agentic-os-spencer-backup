## The Two Pricing Models (and Why the Difference Matters)

Mole control companies in Western Washington use two pricing structures. They look different on a quote. The real difference shows up on your final bill.

### Per-Mole Pricing

Some companies charge a setup fee plus a fee for each mole caught. The quote sounds low — typically $130 for setup plus $80 per mole.

Here's the catch: you don't know how many moles are on your property until the service is done. Most homeowners assume one or two. In reality, the average property in Western Washington has three to five active moles. Some have more.

**What the final bill actually looks like:**

| Moles caught | Setup | Per-mole fee | Total cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 mole | $130 | $80 | **$210** |
| 2 moles | $130 | $160 | **$290** |
| 3 moles | $130 | $240 | **$370** |
| 4 moles | $130 | $320 | **$450** |
| 5 moles | $130 | $400 | **$530** |
| 6 moles | $130 | $480 | **$610** |

At four moles, per-mole pricing matches the flat rate. At five or more, you're paying significantly more — and you had no way of knowing that when you said yes.

The per-mole model also creates a bad incentive. The more moles on your property, the more the company earns. There's no financial reason for them to resolve the problem quickly.

### Flat-Rate Pricing

Got Moles charges **$450 flat rate** for residential properties under 1 acre. The price is the same whether we catch one mole or six. It includes:

- Full property inspection and mole activity mapping
- Professional equipment placement on active tunnels
- 4-5 weekly service visits with adjustments at each visit
- All equipment removal at end of service
- **The guarantee:** a $150 setup fee is collected upfront. If we don't catch a mole during the service period, you pay only the $150 — not the full $450.

No per-mole surcharges. No escalating invoices. No surprise at the end.

## The Hidden Cost of the Cheapest Option

The cheapest approach is always DIY. Traps cost $20-$50. Castor oil costs $15-$30. Total outlay: under $100.

But most DIY approaches fail in Washington. Effective trap placement requires reading mole behavior that takes years of field experience to learn. Without that knowledge, most homeowners put traps in the wrong tunnels and catch nothing.

The real cost of DIY is the three months of frustration, the continued lawn damage, and the professional call-out you make anyway. At that point you've spent $100-$200 on products that didn't work plus the $290-$450 for the professional. You'd have been better off calling from the start.
