## The Legal Landscape

Washington's mole trapping regulations are more complex than most homeowners realise. Two laws pull in different directions:

**RCW 77.36.030** gives property owners the right to "trap or kill moles on their own property when they are causing damage to crops, domestic animals, or their property." This is clear — if moles are wrecking your lawn, you're legally entitled to deal with them.

**Initiative 713**, passed by Washington voters in 2000, restricts the use of "body-gripping traps" for capturing mammals. The law was primarily aimed at fur trapping and wildlife management. The Washington State Attorney General's office issued an opinion in 2002 (AGO 2002 No. 3) interpreting the law's scope — concluding that the definition of body-gripping traps is broad.

Moles are classified as "unclassified" under Washington law — they're not a protected species, and they're not formally classified as wildlife in the way that deer, elk, or bear are. They sit in a grey area that the law wasn't specifically designed to address.

## What This Means for Homeowners

If you're a homeowner trying to deal with moles on your own, here's the practical situation:

**You have the right to remove moles from your property.** RCW 77.36.030 is explicit about this. Property damage from moles gives you the legal standing to act.

**DIY traps are difficult to use effectively regardless of legality.** The real problem with store-bought mole traps isn't just regulation — it's that effective mole trapping requires precise placement in active tunnels at the correct depth. Without years of field experience, most homeowners set traps in inactive runs and catch nothing. The trap is only half the equation. Knowing where to put it is what actually matters.

**This is why professional mole control exists as a specialist trade.** Companies that do this for a living have developed their methods through thousands of jobs. They know which tunnels are active, which direction the mole travels, how deep to go, and how to avoid scent contamination that makes moles bypass a trap entirely.

## Why DIY Mole Control Fails

The trapping law is a factor, but it's not the main reason homeowners fail at mole control. The main reasons are:

**Wrong tunnel.** Moles maintain a network of tunnels — some active, some abandoned. Placing a trap in an abandoned tunnel produces nothing. Professionals can read which tunnels are actively being used based on soil freshness, mound patterns, and run characteristics.

**Wrong depth.** Townsend's moles — the dominant species in Western Washington — dig at different depths depending on the season, soil moisture, and food availability. A trap set too shallow or too deep misses the mole entirely.

**Scent contamination.** Moles detect human scent on traps and avoid them. Professional trappers handle equipment in ways that minimise scent transfer — a detail that most homeowners don't know about and hardware store instructions don't mention.

**Wrong expectations.** Most homeowners assume they have one mole. The average Western Washington property has three to five. A single trap, even well-placed, doesn't address the full problem.

## What About Poison?

Mole poison baits (such as Talpirid) are available in Washington. They're not covered by Initiative 713, which addresses traps specifically. However, poison has significant drawbacks:

- **Safety risk** — poison baits can be ingested by dogs, cats, and children
- **Inconsistent results** — moles are selective feeders and often ignore bait entirely
- **No confirmation** — unlike trapping, there's no way to know the mole was actually eliminated
- **Environmental concern** — secondary poisoning affects predators that eat poisoned moles
