## SITEWIDE CHANGES

### 1. One-Time Service → Quick Fix Program

**Spencer's request:** Find/replace all instances of "one-time service" / "one-time removal" / "one-time mole removal" with "Quick Fix Program" sitewide.

**Status: APPROVE + SEO FIX**

**SEO/GEO Risk: HIGH**

"One-time mole removal" is a real search phrase homeowners use. "Quick Fix Program" is branded terminology — nobody Googles it. A straight find/replace kills organic discovery for this service.

**Recommendation:**
- Use "Quick Fix Program" as the brand name in headings, CTAs, and navigation — Spencer's branding is correct for conversion
- BUT keep "one-time mole removal" as descriptive text within body copy on the Quick Fix page itself
- Add a definition-lead sentence for GEO: *"Our Quick Fix Program is a one-time mole removal service designed for homeowners who need fast results without a long-term commitment."*
- Page title tag: `Quick Fix Program — One-Time Mole Removal | Got Moles?`
- H1 can be "Quick Fix Program", but the first sentence must connect the brand name to the search term
- Schema: keep `Service` type with `name: "One-Time Mole Removal"` and `alternateName: "Quick Fix Program"`

**Why this matters:** Google and AI models need the descriptive phrase to understand what the page is about. The branded name alone tells them nothing. This approach gives Spencer his brand name AND keeps the SEO equity.

**Counter-text to add:**
- Quick Fix page intro: "Our Quick Fix Program is Got Moles' one-time mole removal service — fast, professional, and backed by our results guarantee."
- Any CTA that says "Quick Fix Program" should have supporting text nearby that says "one-time mole removal" naturally

## STRATEGIC DISCUSSION

### 30. Blog Content Strategy — DIY Depth

**Spencer's concern:** How much DIY detail should the blog provide? Risk of enabling customers to solve the problem themselves.

**Status: PUSHBACK — RECOMMEND THE MIDDLE GROUND**

**SEO/GEO Analysis:**

Spencer's instinct is partially right — detailed step-by-step trap instructions DO enable DIY. But the middle ground in his brief is exactly correct, and here's the data to back it up:

**The case for educational (not instructional) content:**
1. "How to get rid of moles" is the HIGHEST volume keyword in the entire mole control space. Ignoring it means ceding that traffic to competitors.
2. AI models (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude) answer "how to get rid of moles" queries by citing authoritative sources. Got Moles should BE that source.
3. The conversion path is: read educational content → realize it's harder than expected → call Got Moles. This is proven across every home services vertical.

**The content framework (EDUCATE, DON'T INSTRUCT):**
- **YES:** "Why trapping is the only method that actually works" (positions Got Moles' approach)
- **YES:** "5 reasons DIY mole removal fails" (builds the case for professional service)
- **YES:** "What to expect from professional mole removal" (pre-sells the service)
- **YES:** "Mole myths debunked" (authority building)
- **NO:** "Step-by-step: how to set a snap trap" (this is Checkmate Trap Co. content)
- **NO:** "Best mole traps ranked" (product content, not service content)

**Recommendation:** Write blog content that makes homeowners SMARTER about the problem but doesn't hand them the tools to solve it. Position Got Moles as the expert who explains WHY things work, not HOW to do them yourself. Reserve the detailed DIY playbook for Checkmate Trap Co. where it becomes a product sales funnel instead of a service-cannibalization risk.

Spencer's suggested middle ground is exactly right. Endorse it and proceed.
